## Phase 1: Strategic Diagnosis

### 1.1 Environment Scan

Three shifts in our operating environment deserve attention heading into Q3:

**AI-driven commoditization of basic research.** Clients are increasingly using AI tools to generate competitive landscapes, market sizing estimates, and trend summaries that they used to pay us $15-25K per engagement to produce. Two Q2 clients explicitly told us they cancelled planned projects because "ChatGPT got us 80% of the way there." This does not threaten our deep-dive work, but it is eroding the entry-level engagements that historically fed our pipeline.

**Forrester's acquisition of NarrowBridge Analytics.** Forrester closed its acquisition of NarrowBridge in April, absorbing a 12-person boutique that overlapped heavily with our SaaS competitive intelligence practice. NarrowBridge's clients are being migrated to Forrester's platform. This matters because (a) it removes a peer-level competitor we sometimes lost to, and (b) it means Forrester now has a dedicated CI offering that competes with our flagship service at a much larger scale.

**Tech sector budget compression.** Series B and C SaaS companies -- our core client segment -- are entering a second year of tighter operating budgets. CFOs are scrutinizing every discretionary line item. Our average deal cycle lengthened from 3.2 weeks to 5.1 weeks in Q2, and we saw two signed SOWs get "paused" after procurement review.

### 1.2 Competitive Position (Key Forces)

Of Porter's Five Forces, two are reshaping our competitive position right now:

**Threat of substitutes (HIGH and rising).** AI tools are the substitute. They don't replace what we do at the top end, but they are compressing the value of our lower-tier offerings. Clients can now self-serve on basic competitive intelligence. This force is structural and accelerating.

**Competitive rivalry (MODERATE, shifting).** The boutique tier is consolidating (NarrowBridge is gone). The remaining independents are either hyper-specialized or struggling. Meanwhile, the large analyst firms are moving downstream with productized CI offerings. We sit in an uncomfortable middle -- too small to match Forrester's breadth, not differentiated enough to be the obvious specialist pick in any single niche. Rivalry is moderate today but the squeeze is tightening.

Buyer power is elevated due to budget pressure but is cyclical rather than structural. Supplier power and threat of new entrants are not material factors for us right now.

### 1.4 Strategic Diagnosis

**Core challenge:** Apex Insights is a strong team selling an increasingly commoditized entry-level service to an increasingly cost-conscious client base, while lacking the documented processes, pipeline visibility, and clear specialization needed to move decisively upmarket. We have the talent to win at the high end of competitive intelligence for B2B SaaS, but we haven't built the operational infrastructure or market positioning to consistently get there. The next 90 days need to close the gap between our capability and our positioning before the market squeeze forces a more painful correction.

## Phase 2: Strategic Direction

### 2.1 Winning Aspiration

*Be the firm that B2B SaaS companies call when the strategic question is too important to get wrong.*

This hasn't changed. It still fits. The emphasis on "too important to get wrong" is more relevant than ever -- it's the exact space AI tools can't occupy.

### 2.2 Where to Play

**Client segments we're choosing:**
- Series B+ B2B SaaS companies ($10M-$200M ARR) making high-stakes strategic decisions: market entry, competitive repositioning, M&A due diligence, pricing strategy
- Private equity firms evaluating B2B SaaS investments (diligence work)
- We will also serve larger enterprises ($200M+) but only for discrete SaaS market questions, not as a general-purpose research vendor

**Where NOT to play:**
- Basic competitive landscape reports (the AI-commoditized tier). We will stop offering the "Market Snapshot" package after honoring existing commitments.
- Consumer tech, hardware, or non-SaaS markets. We've taken opportunistic projects here before. No more.
- Ongoing retainer-based monitoring services. We've been asked about this repeatedly. It's a different business model that would dilute our focus.
- Clients below $5M ARR. The deal economics don't work at our price point, and these clients need generalists, not specialists.

**Service types we're choosing:**
- Deep competitive intelligence (our flagship -- 4-6 week engagements)
- Strategic market entry assessments
- M&A commercial due diligence for PE firms
- Executive strategy workshops (new offering, piloting in Q3)

**Geography:** North America. We've had inbound from European SaaS companies. Not yet -- the time zones and regulatory context add complexity we're not staffed for.

### 2.3 How to Win

**Primary advantage: Deep specialization in B2B SaaS competitive dynamics.**

We will win by knowing the B2B SaaS competitive landscape better than any firm our size or larger. This means maintaining active coverage of 200+ SaaS companies across our focus verticals, building proprietary frameworks for SaaS competitive analysis, and having every analyst deeply fluent in SaaS business models, metrics, and strategy.

**Supporting advantage: Senior-led delivery.**

Every engagement is led by a partner or senior analyst who has direct client contact. Clients get the A-team, not a junior associate supervised from a distance. This is the opposite of how large analyst firms operate and it's the #1 reason clients choose us over Forrester or Gartner.

**Key capabilities needed to execute this strategy:**

1. **Proprietary SaaS competitive intelligence database.** We need a structured, queryable repository of our accumulated research. This is our moat -- every engagement should make the next one faster and more insightful.
2. **Repeatable delivery methodology.** Documented frameworks, templates, and quality gates so we can onboard a new senior analyst and have them leading engagements within 8 weeks, not 6 months.
3. **PE relationship pipeline.** Due diligence work is high-margin, repeat-purchase, and counter-cyclical. We need 3-5 active PE relationships, not the 1 we have today.
4. **Thought leadership engine.** One substantive piece per month that demonstrates our depth. Not content marketing fluff -- actual analysis that a VP of Strategy would forward to their CEO.

### 2.4 Strategic Option: Launch Executive Strategy Workshops

We're considering adding a half-day workshop format where we present competitive intelligence findings and facilitate strategic decision-making with client leadership teams. Let's stress-test this using the Reverse Engineering method.

**What would have to be true for this to succeed?**

| Condition | Current Assessment | Confidence |
|-----------|--------------------|------------|
| Clients value facilitated strategy sessions over just receiving a report | Sarah has heard this request from 3 clients in the last 6 months. Plausible but unvalidated at scale. | Medium |
| We have the facilitation skills to run a high-stakes exec session | Marcus and Sarah have done this informally. Neither has formal facilitation training. Gap is closable but real. | Medium |
| We can price this at $8-12K incremental on top of a standard engagement | Comparable offerings from strategy firms run $15-25K standalone. Our bundled price seems reasonable. | Medium-High |
| It doesn't cannibalize billable research hours | A half-day workshop plus prep is ~2 days of analyst time. At current utilization, we can absorb 1-2 per month. More than that creates a capacity problem. | High |
| Clients will actually get their leadership team in a room for half a day | This is the riskiest condition. Executive calendars are brutal. Remote option may be necessary. | Low-Medium |

**Assessment:** The biggest barrier is the last condition -- getting executives in the room. We should pilot this with 2 existing clients who've already expressed interest, offer a remote option, and evaluate before committing to a formal launch. Adding it to the website as a service offering is premature.

### 3.3 Issues Identified, Discussed, and Solved (IDS)

**Issue 1: Should we formally sunset the Market Snapshot offering?**

- **Identify:** The Market Snapshot ($8-12K, 1-week turnaround, basic competitive landscape) accounts for ~20% of Q2 revenue but is being commoditized by AI tools. Margins are thin and declining. However, it's also how 40% of our larger engagements start -- clients try the Snapshot first, then come back for the deep dive.
- **Discuss:** Sarah argued we can't cut it cold because of its feeder function. Marcus countered that the feeder function is declining -- of the last 8 Snapshot clients, only 1 converted to a deep engagement. Priya suggested we replace it with a free or low-cost "SaaS Competitive Briefing" that demonstrates our depth without the full production cost.
- **Solve:** We will stop selling Market Snapshots as a standalone paid offering effective August 1. In its place, we will create a streamlined "Competitive Briefing" (AI-assisted, 1 day of analyst time) that we offer free to qualified prospects as a conversion tool. Sarah will track conversion rates. If the new format doesn't convert at 25%+ within 90 days, we revisit.

**Issue 2: Our website doesn't reflect our strategic direction.**

- **Identify:** The website still positions us as a generalist market research firm. It lists consumer tech and hardware as service areas. The case studies are outdated. A prospect visiting our site would not understand that we specialize in B2B SaaS competitive intelligence.
- **Discuss:** Everyone agreed this is a problem but nobody has bandwidth to project-manage a redesign. We considered hiring a freelance web designer but past experience was mixed.
- **Solve:** This is not a Rock -- it's a To-Do with a deadline. Sarah will hire a contract copywriter by July 15 to rewrite the core pages (Home, Services, About, 2 case studies). Marcus will provide the case study content. Priya will manage the copywriter. Target: updated site live by August 15. Design stays the same; this is a messaging overhaul, not a redesign.

**Issue 3: Marcus is the single point of failure for client delivery.**

- **Identify:** Every engagement goes through Marcus for quality review and most for primary analysis. If Marcus is sick for a week, we miss deadlines. This also means Marcus can't take vacation.
- **Discuss:** This is partly addressed by Rock 2 (documenting methodology) and Rock 4 (hiring a senior analyst). But even with both, Marcus will remain the quality bottleneck unless we change the review process.
- **Solve:** Priya will begin shadowing Marcus on quality reviews starting in July. By end of Q3, Priya should be able to independently approve deliverables for standard engagements (not M&A due diligence). Marcus retains final review on PE/M&A work and any engagement over $50K.

## Phase 4: Validation

### 4.1 Coherence Check

### 4.2 Concerns and Responses

**Concern raised by Priya:** "We're asking Marcus to build a CI database, pilot workshops, AND continue leading all client engagements. Even with me taking on more review responsibility, this feels like too much. What gives if a big engagement lands in August?"

**Response:** Valid concern. If a new engagement of $40K+ lands, Rock 5 (workshop pilot) moves to the back of the priority stack and may slip into Q4. Rock 1 (CI database) has flexibility in scope -- we'll target 150 profiles but 100 is still a successful quarter. The non-negotiable is Rock 4 (hiring) because it's the bottleneck for everything else. Sarah will prioritize the hire above Rock 3 if there's a conflict.

**Concern raised by Sarah:** "The PE outreach target of 15 firms feels arbitrary. I'd rather do 8 highly targeted introductions than 15 cold emails."

**Response:** Agreed. Revised target: outreach to 10 firms, with at least 6 being warm introductions through existing network. Quality over volume.
