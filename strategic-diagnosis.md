## 1. Diagnosis

_What is the core challenge facing the business right now? A good diagnosis simplifies the complexity of the situation by identifying the one or two critical factors that matter most._

> [Identify what creates the difficulty — competitive dynamics, internal capability gaps, market shifts, resource constraints.]

## 2. Guiding Policy

_The guiding policy is the overall approach chosen to deal with the challenge identified in the diagnosis. It channels action in certain directions without defining exactly what to do._

**Given the diagnosis, what is our overall approach?**

> [Describe the approach — e.g., "Focus all growth resources on the mid-market segment where we have a proven advantage, and stop pursuing enterprise deals that drain resources."]

**What are we choosing NOT to do?**

> [Good strategy requires saying no. What are you deliberately setting aside?]

**What advantage or leverage does this policy exploit?**

> [Where is the asymmetry? What makes this approach work for you specifically?]

> [A clear, directive statement of how you will approach the challenge.]

## Bad Strategy Checklist

_Review your work above against these common failures. Check any that apply and revise._

- [ ] **Fluff.** Does any part of this use buzzwords or inflated language that says nothing? ("Leveraging synergies to drive customer-centric innovation.")
- [ ] **Failure to face the challenge.** Does the diagnosis actually name the hard problem, or does it dance around it?
- [ ] **Mistaking goals for strategy.** Does the guiding policy describe an approach, or just restate a desired outcome? ("Our strategy is to grow revenue 20%" is not a strategy.)
- [ ] **Bad strategic objectives.** Are the actions a wishlist of disconnected goals, or a focused, coherent set of moves?
- [ ] **Dog's dinner objectives.** Are there too many priorities? (If everything is a priority, nothing is.)
