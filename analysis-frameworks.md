## 1. Rumelt's Strategic Diagnosis

Source: Richard Rumelt, *Good Strategy Bad Strategy*

Rumelt's core claim: the vast majority of what passes for strategy is bad strategy. Good strategy is rare because it requires the hard work of figuring out what is actually going on and making difficult choices. Start every strategic planning session here.

### The Kernel of Good Strategy

Every good strategy has three elements. All three must be present and must connect logically.

**Element 1: Diagnosis**

The diagnosis simplifies the overwhelming complexity of reality by identifying which aspects of the situation are critical. It is a judgment call -- not a list of problems, but a narrative that names the one or two factors that matter most.

How to produce a diagnosis:

1. Ask: **"What's going on here?"** Write down everything that comes to mind. Do not filter.
2. Ask it again: **"No, really -- what's actually going on here?"** Push past the surface symptoms to underlying dynamics.
3. Ask it a third time: **"What is the most fundamental challenge we face?"** Force yourself to synthesize multiple observations into one coherent challenge statement.
4. Test the diagnosis by completing this sentence: "The core challenge facing CTRS right now is ___, which means that ___." The second clause should make the implications clear.
5. Verify that the diagnosis is genuinely simplifying. If it contains more than two "and" conjunctions, it is still a list, not a diagnosis.

A good diagnosis:
- Names the nature of the challenge, not just its symptoms
- Identifies the one or two factors with the most leverage
- Creates a domain of action -- makes it clear what kind of response is needed
- Often reframes the problem in a way that makes the path forward more apparent

Example for a firm like CTRS: "The core challenge is that our revenue concentration in a single client makes every strategic decision a survival decision, which means we cannot invest in growth until we diversify our client base."

**Element 2: Guiding Policy**

The guiding policy is the overall approach chosen to cope with or overcome the challenge identified in the diagnosis. It is not a goal or a vision. It is a direction -- a decision about what kind of response you will pursue.

- It channels and constrains action without defining every step
- It rules out a wide range of possible actions
- It creates advantage by anticipating the actions of others or exploiting a source of leverage

To formulate: Complete the sentence: "To address this challenge, our approach is to ___." If the answer could apply to any firm in any situation (e.g., "leverage our strengths" or "pursue excellence"), it is not a guiding policy. It must be specific enough that someone could disagree with it.

**Element 3: Coherent Actions**

Coherent actions are the coordinated set of moves that carry out the guiding policy. "Coherent" is the operative word. The actions must reinforce each other, not work at cross purposes.

Test for coherence:
- Do the actions all serve the guiding policy?
- Do they reinforce each other? (Does doing action A make action B more effective?)
- Are resources concentrated, or spread thinly across unrelated initiatives?
- Is there anything on the action list that contradicts the guiding policy?

### The Four Hallmarks of Bad Strategy

Use these as a diagnostic checklist. If any of these are present in your current strategic plan, flag them immediately.

**Hallmark 1: Fluff**

Fluff is a restatement of the obvious, combined with buzzwords that create the illusion of expertise. It uses inflated language to disguise the absence of thought.

Test: Read your strategy statement aloud. If removing the jargon leaves an empty sentence, it is fluff. "We will leverage our synergistic capabilities to create value for stakeholders" says nothing. "We will win financial services clients by delivering competitive intelligence reports faster than any competitor" says something.

**Hallmark 2: Failure to Face the Challenge**

Bad strategy avoids naming the core problem because naming it forces uncomfortable choices. If a strategy does not define the challenge, there is no way to evaluate whether the approach is right.

Test: Can you state the specific obstacle the strategy is designed to overcome? If the strategy is just a list of desirable outcomes without naming what stands in the way, it fails this test.

**Hallmark 3: Mistaking Goals for Strategy**

Goals are not strategy. "Grow revenue 20%" is a goal. "Win three retainer clients in financial services by specializing our competitive intelligence methodology for asset managers" is a strategy. The difference: strategy includes the logic of how.

Test: Does the plan contain a theory of why these actions will succeed? Or is it just targets with enthusiasm?

**Hallmark 4: Bad Strategic Objectives**

Bad strategic objectives are either (a) too vague to guide action, (b) impractical given resources, or (c) a long wish list that fails to address the core challenge. A "dog's dinner" list of objectives -- a little of this, a little of that -- is a clear sign.

Test: Would accomplishing all stated objectives actually resolve the diagnosed challenge? If the objectives could all be achieved and the fundamental problem would remain, the objectives are bad.

### Sources of Power

These are the mechanisms through which strategy creates advantage. When formulating your guiding policy and actions, draw on whichever sources are available to CTRS.

**Leverage**

Apply strength at the point of greatest effect. Identify the pivot point -- the place where concentrated effort produces disproportionate results.

For CTRS, ask:
- Where does a small investment of our time create the most client value?
- Which client relationship, if deepened, would open the most doors?
- Which capability improvement would make everything else we do more effective?

**Proximate Objectives**

Set objectives that are close enough to be feasible. An objective is proximate when you can reasonably envision how to accomplish it and can tell whether it has been achieved. "Dominate the market intelligence space" is not proximate. "Win the Smith & Co. engagement by demonstrating our sector expertise in the proposal next Tuesday" is proximate.

When ambiguity is high, set the most proximate objective you can: something achievable in days or weeks that resolves uncertainty and opens up the next move.

**Chain-Link Systems**

Performance in some systems is limited by the weakest link. If a chain-link situation exists, improving any single link other than the weakest produces zero improvement. Identify whether CTRS has chain-link dynamics.

For a consulting firm, common chain-link patterns:
- Lead generation --> proposal quality --> delivery quality --> client retention. If delivery is weak, improving lead generation just increases the number of clients you disappoint.
- Research methodology --> analyst skill --> report writing --> client presentation. A brilliant analysis communicated poorly is wasted.

Diagnose: Which link is weakest? Focus all improvement effort there until it is no longer the bottleneck.

**Focus**

Coordinate policies and actions to support one focused position. Focus means saying no. List the things CTRS is currently doing. For each, ask: "Does this directly advance our guiding policy?" Remove or deprioritize anything that does not.

**Using Advantage**

Advantage comes from an asymmetry between competitors -- something you have or can do that others cannot easily replicate. Identify CTRS's asymmetries:
- Proprietary access to data or sources?
- Deep relationships with specific decision-makers?
- Specialized methodology that took years to build?
- Reputation in a specific niche?

Then ask: Are we deepening this advantage or dissipating it by spreading into areas where we have no edge?

**Using Dynamics**

Ride waves of change rather than fighting them. When the competitive landscape shifts (new technology, regulatory change, market disruption), the firms that see the shift first and reposition gain disproportionate advantage.

For CTRS, ask:
- What technological, regulatory, or market shifts are currently underway?
- Are we positioned to benefit from them, or will they erode our position?
- Can we be the first to adopt a new approach that others will follow?

**Inertia and Entropy**

Competitors and clients are often trapped by inertia -- the unwillingness or inability to change. Entropy causes organizations to drift toward mediocrity over time without active management.

Exploit competitor inertia: Large firms that compete with CTRS are slow. Their processes are rigid, their pricing is inflexible, their people are interchangeable. Where are they stuck?

Guard against your own entropy: What has CTRS stopped doing well that it used to do well? What processes have decayed? Where has "good enough" replaced "excellent"?

## 2. Porter's Five Forces

Source: Michael Porter, *Competitive Strategy*

Use this framework to assess the structural attractiveness of the market segments CTRS competes in (or is considering entering). The five forces determine the profit potential of an industry. For CTRS, apply this at the level of the specific service/client segment, not "consulting" broadly.

### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants

How easy is it for new competitors to enter your market and take your clients?

Sub-factors to evaluate:
- **Capital requirements**: Low for market intelligence -- a laptop and expertise is enough. This makes the threat high.
- **Economies of scale**: Do larger firms have a meaningful cost advantage? For bespoke intelligence work, usually not much.
- **Switching costs for clients**: How hard is it for clients to change providers? If your work is project-based, switching costs are low. If you hold institutional knowledge about a client's competitive landscape, switching costs are higher.
- **Access to distribution channels**: How do clients find providers? If through personal relationships, new entrants face barriers. If through RFPs or procurement, barriers are low.
- **Brand identity/reputation**: Does CTRS have name recognition that a newcomer cannot quickly replicate?
- **Proprietary methodology or technology**: Do you have tools, databases, or methods that are hard to copy?
- **Regulatory barriers**: Licensing, certifications, clearances that restrict entry.

**For CTRS, pay special attention to**: Barriers based on relationships and specialized knowledge are the strongest entry barriers a small firm can build. Generic "market research" has very low barriers. Specialized intelligence in specific sectors has moderate barriers.

### Force 2: Bargaining Power of Buyers (Clients)

How much power do your clients have to push down your prices or demand more for less?

Sub-factors:
- **Buyer concentration**: If a few large clients represent most of your revenue, their power is very high.
- **Buyer volume**: Large-volume buyers have more leverage.
- **Switching costs**: Low switching costs give buyers power.
- **Buyer information**: If buyers know your costs and margins, they can pressure pricing.
- **Price sensitivity**: How important is price relative to quality in buyer decisions?
- **Threat of backward integration**: Could the client build an internal team to do what you do?
- **Product differentiation**: If your work is seen as interchangeable with competitors', buyer power is high.

**For CTRS, this is typically the most important force.** A small firm with concentrated revenue is highly vulnerable to buyer power. Track: What percentage of revenue comes from the top client? Top three? If any single client exceeds 30%, this is a strategic risk that must be addressed.

### Force 3: Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Who supplies the inputs you need, and how much power do they have?

For a market intelligence firm, "suppliers" include:
- **Talent**: Skilled analysts are the primary input. If they are scarce, their bargaining power is high.
- **Data providers**: Bloomberg, Statista, proprietary databases, etc. If there is only one source for a critical data input, that supplier has power.
- **Technology vendors**: AI/ML tools, research platforms, CRM systems.
- **Subcontractors/freelancers**: Subject matter experts engaged for specific projects.

Evaluate: Can any supplier significantly raise prices or reduce quality without you having an alternative? If yes, that supplier has power.

### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes

What alternatives exist to hiring CTRS that address the same client need?

Substitutes for market intelligence services include:
- **Internal teams**: Clients building their own competitive intelligence function
- **AI-powered tools**: Automated competitive monitoring platforms (Crayon, Klue, Contify)
- **Management consulting firms**: Offering strategic analysis as part of broader engagements
- **Free information**: Clients deciding they can get "good enough" intelligence from public sources themselves
- **Adjacent service providers**: Strategy firms, investment banks, industry analysts who provide intelligence as a byproduct
- **Doing nothing**: Clients deciding they don't need competitive intelligence at all

**For CTRS, the AI/automation substitute threat deserves special ongoing monitoring.** Assess quarterly: What parts of our work could clients plausibly replace with tools? What parts require human judgment that tools cannot replicate?

### Force 5: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

How intense is competition among firms currently serving your target clients?

Sub-factors:
- **Number and balance of competitors**: Many equally sized competitors means high rivalry.
- **Industry growth rate**: In slow-growth markets, firms fight over the same clients. In growing markets, everyone can grow without taking share.
- **Fixed costs / value added**: If fixed costs are high (which they aren't for a small firm), pressure to fill capacity increases rivalry.
- **Product differentiation**: If services are seen as commodities, competition defaults to price.
- **Diversity of competitors**: When competitors have different strategies, origins, and personalities, rivalry is less predictable.
- **Exit barriers**: When firms cannot easily exit, they stay and fight even when returns are poor.

Assess: Who are the 3-5 firms most likely to compete with CTRS for the same engagements? How do they compete -- on price, quality, relationships, speed, breadth, specialization?

### Five Forces Summary Assessment

After evaluating each force, rate it: **Low / Moderate / High** pressure on profitability. Then answer:

1. Which one or two forces most constrain CTRS's profitability right now?
2. Can CTRS's strategy reduce the pressure of these forces? (e.g., deepening specialization reduces buyer power and rivalry; building proprietary tools raises entry barriers)
3. Are the forces getting stronger or weaker over time?

## 3. PESTEL Analysis

Source: Standard strategic planning framework, adapted here for CTRS Market Intelligence.

PESTEL scans the macro-environment for factors that could create opportunities or threats. Do not attempt to be exhaustive. Focus on factors that have a realistic chance of affecting CTRS in the next 1-3 years. Spend no more than 20 minutes on this scan unless a major shift is underway.

### Political

- Are there changes in government policy that affect the industries CTRS serves? (Changes in defense policy, trade policy, regulatory enforcement that drive demand for intelligence?)
- Are government contracts or grants available for market intelligence work?
- How does the political environment affect client budgets? (Uncertainty often increases demand for intelligence; austerity reduces consulting budgets.)
- Are there geopolitical shifts creating new intelligence needs? (Trade wars, sanctions, reshoring?)

### Economic

- What is the current economic cycle doing to client buying behavior? (In downturns, clients cut discretionary consulting first. In upturns, they invest in competitive positioning.)
- How are interest rates and capital availability affecting client industries? (Tight capital = less M&A = less due diligence work. Easy capital = more M&A = more demand.)
- What is the talent market like for analysts? (Tight labor market = higher compensation costs. Loose market = opportunity to hire.)
- Are clients consolidating? (Fewer, larger clients means higher buyer power.)
- What is happening with consulting industry pricing? (Are rates rising, falling, or stable?)

### Social

- Are decision-makers at client firms changing their expectations for how intelligence is delivered? (Faster? More visual? More actionable? Integrated with their workflows?)
- Is there a generational shift in how clients buy professional services? (Younger buyers may expect self-service dashboards, not PDF reports.)
- Are remote/hybrid work patterns affecting how CTRS operates or how clients engage?
- Is there a shift in the perceived value of "expert opinion" vs. "data-driven analysis"?

### Technological

**This is typically the most important PESTEL category for a market intelligence firm.**

- How are AI and large language models changing the market intelligence landscape? What tasks can AI now do that previously required an analyst? What new capabilities does AI enable?
- Are new data sources becoming available that change what is possible? (Alternative data, satellite imagery, web scraping advances, social listening tools?)
- Are clients adopting new platforms where CTRS needs to deliver? (BI tools, collaboration platforms, decision-support systems?)
- What technology investments would most improve CTRS's productivity and output quality?
- Are competitors adopting technology faster than CTRS?

### Environmental/Ecological

- Are ESG reporting requirements creating demand for related intelligence services?
- Are sustainability concerns affecting the industries CTRS serves in ways that create new intelligence needs?
- Are there climate-related disruptions to the industries CTRS covers?
- (For most market intelligence firms, this category is lower priority. Flag and move on unless a genuine factor exists.)

### Legal

- Are there changes to data privacy laws that affect how CTRS collects or handles information? (GDPR, CCPA, emerging AI regulation?)
- Are there intellectual property considerations for CTRS's methodologies or outputs?
- Are non-compete or non-solicitation trends affecting talent acquisition?
- Are there changes to contractor/employment law that affect how CTRS engages freelancers or subcontractors?
- Is there emerging regulation of AI-generated content that could affect CTRS's work product?

### PESTEL Output

Summarize in a table:

| Category | Key Factor | Impact on CTRS | Timeframe | Action Required? |
|----------|-----------|---------------|-----------|-----------------|
| T | AI tools replacing basic research tasks | High -- threatens lower-value work, enables higher-value work | Now | Yes |
| E | Client budget tightening in tech sector | Moderate -- affects 40% of pipeline | 6-12 months | Monitor |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

Keep only factors rated moderate or high impact. Discard the rest.

## 4. EOS Organizational Checkup

Source: Gino Wickman, *Traction: Get a Grip on Your Business*

The Entrepreneurial Operating System (EOS) provides a practical framework for assessing organizational health. Use this during the internal assessment phase of quarterly planning. Rate each component honestly -- this is a diagnostic tool, not a performance review.

### The Six Key Components

Rate each component from 1 (weak/nonexistent) to 10 (fully functioning). A score below 8 requires attention. Be honest -- inflated scores defeat the purpose.

**1. Vision (Target: everyone aligned on where you're going)**

Rate these questions 1-10:
- Can every team member articulate where CTRS is going and how it plans to get there?
- Is there a written Vision/Traction Organizer (V/TO) that the team references?
- Does the team agree on core values and use them in hiring and firing decisions?
- Is there a clear, compelling 10-year target?
- Is the 3-year picture specific enough to be useful?
- Does everyone know the 1-year plan and current quarter's priorities?

**2. People (Target: right people in right seats)**

Rate these questions 1-10:
- Does every person in the firm share CTRS's core values? (Right people)
- Does every person GWC their role -- Get it, Want it, have the Capacity to do it? (Right seats)
- Is the accountability chart clear? Does everyone know who owns what?
- When someone is in the wrong seat, is the issue addressed promptly or allowed to linger?
- Is there a clear process for evaluating performance?

**3. Data (Target: managing by a handful of key numbers)**

Rate these questions 1-10:
- Does CTRS have a weekly scorecard with 5-15 activity-based numbers?
- Can you look at the scorecard and tell within 15 minutes whether the business is on track?
- Does every person have at least one measurable number they own?
- Are you tracking leading indicators (pipeline, proposals, activities) or only lagging indicators (revenue, profit)?
- Is data collected consistently and reviewed weekly?

**4. Issues (Target: issues identified and resolved systematically)**

Rate these questions 1-10:
- Does CTRS maintain a running issues list?
- Are issues resolved using a disciplined process (like IDS: Identify, Discuss, Solve)?
- Do the same issues keep coming back, or are they resolved permanently?
- Is the team comfortable raising uncomfortable issues openly?
- Are issues tackled in priority order, or does the loudest voice win?

**5. Process (Target: core processes documented and followed)**

Rate these questions 1-10:
- Are CTRS's 3-5 core processes identified and named? (e.g., client engagement process, research methodology, sales process, hiring process)
- Is each core process documented at a high level (20% that produces 80%)?
- Does the team follow the documented processes consistently?
- When a process breaks, is it fixed at the system level or patched with heroic effort?
- Could a new hire learn the core processes from documentation?

**6. Traction (Target: executing consistently with discipline and accountability)**

Rate these questions 1-10:
- Does CTRS set quarterly Rocks (3-7 priorities) and complete 80%+ of them?
- Is there a weekly meeting pulse (Level 10 meetings)?
- Do meetings follow a standard agenda and end with clear to-dos?
- Are commitments tracked and is follow-through the norm?
- Is there a cadence: annual planning --> quarterly planning --> weekly meetings --> daily huddles?

### The V/TO: Vision/Traction Organizer

The V/TO captures strategy on two pages using Eight Questions. Complete or update this during every quarterly planning session.

**The Eight Questions:**

1. **What are your core values?** (3-7 values that define who you are. Use for hiring, firing, and decision-making. These do not change.)

2. **What is your core focus?** Two parts:
   - **Purpose/Cause/Passion**: Why does CTRS exist beyond making money?
   - **Niche**: What does CTRS do better than anyone else? (This must be narrow enough to be credible.)

3. **What is your 10-Year Target?** A single, bold, long-range goal. Specific enough to know when you've hit it. This is Collins's BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal).

4. **What is your marketing strategy?** Four parts:
   - **Target market**: Who is your ideal client? (Demographics, firmographics, psychographics.)
   - **Three Uniques**: What three things make CTRS unique in combination? (No single unique may be truly unique -- the combination must be.)
   - **Proven process**: What is your named methodology? (Clients buy a process, not just deliverables.)
   - **Guarantee**: What do you guarantee? (Even an implied guarantee. This forces clarity about what you actually promise.)

5. **What is your 3-Year Picture?** Describe what CTRS looks like three years from now. Include: revenue, profit, number of people, number of clients, types of engagements, capabilities, reputation, physical/virtual presence. Be specific.

6. **What is your 1-Year Plan?** Revenue target, profit target, 3-7 specific goals for the year. Each goal must be measurable and have a deadline.

7. **What are your quarterly Rocks?** The 3-7 most important things to accomplish in the next 90 days. Each Rock has one owner and a specific, measurable outcome. (See execution-implementation.md for Rock-setting process.)

8. **What are your Issues?** The master list of all obstacles, ideas, and concerns. Not prioritized yet -- just captured. Prioritize during IDS sessions.

### Using the Checkup Results

After rating all six components:
- Identify the weakest component. This is the chain-link bottleneck (see Rumelt above).
- Identify the component with the greatest gap between importance and current score.
- Set 1-2 quarterly Rocks specifically aimed at strengthening the weakest component.
- Do not try to improve all six at once. Focus.

## 5. Porter's Competitor Analysis Framework

Source: Michael Porter, *Competitive Strategy*

Use this framework when you need to understand a specific competitor's likely behavior -- especially before making a strategic move that will affect them. The goal is to build a Competitor Response Profile: a prediction of how each key competitor will act and react.

### The Four Diagnostic Components

For each competitor that matters (typically 3-5 for CTRS), analyze:

**Component 1: Future Goals**

What is this competitor trying to achieve? Understanding their goals predicts what moves they will initiate and how they will respond to yours.

Questions to answer:
- What are their stated growth ambitions? (Public statements, job postings, press releases)
- Are they optimizing for revenue, profit, market share, or reputation?
- Are they trying to enter new markets or deepen existing ones?
- What is their risk tolerance? (Aggressive growth or conservative?)
- Are they satisfied with their current position? (Satisfied competitors are less reactive.)
- What are the personal goals of their leaders? (Building to sell? Building a legacy? Just drawing a salary?)

**Component 2: Current Strategy**

How is the competitor currently competing? This reveals their operating model and the basis on which they believe they win.

Questions to answer:
- What services do they offer and to whom?
- How do they price? (Premium, competitive, discount, value-based?)
- How do they go to market? (Referrals, content marketing, events, partnerships, cold outreach?)
- What is their delivery model? (Senior-led, leveraged, technology-enabled?)
- What is their geographic scope?
- What is their stated or implicit value proposition?
- Have they recently changed their strategy? In what direction?

**Component 3: Assumptions**

What does the competitor believe about itself, the industry, and the competitive landscape? Assumptions create blind spots.

Questions to answer:
- What does this competitor believe about its own strengths? (They may overestimate or underestimate themselves.)
- What do they believe about the direction of the market? (If they believe the market is consolidating, they will act differently than if they believe it is fragmenting.)
- What conventional wisdom do they follow that might be wrong?
- Do they have historical biases? (e.g., "We always compete on price" or "Clients only buy from big firms")
- What do they believe about CTRS? (If they don't see you as a threat, you have room to maneuver.)

**Component 4: Capabilities**

What can the competitor actually do? Capabilities determine which strategic options are available to them and which responses are feasible.

Assess in these areas:
- **Core competencies**: What are they genuinely good at?
- **Weaknesses**: Where are they vulnerable?
- **Financial resources**: Can they sustain a price war? Fund investment? Absorb losses?
- **Talent**: Quality and depth of their team. Key person dependencies.
- **Technology**: Tools, platforms, proprietary systems.
- **Client relationships**: Depth and breadth of relationships. Contractual lock-in.
- **Capacity**: Can they take on more work, or are they stretched?
- **Speed**: How quickly can they respond to market changes or client requests?

### Building the Competitor Response Profile

Synthesize the four components into predictions:

1. **Offensive moves**: What strategic moves is this competitor likely to make in the next 6-12 months? (Based on their goals and capabilities.)

2. **Defensive reactions**: If CTRS takes a specific action (enters a new market, undercuts on price, launches a new service), how will this competitor respond? Rate the response as:
   - **Unlikely**: The move doesn't threaten their goals or they lack the capability to respond
   - **Slow/weak**: They will respond eventually but without urgency or effectiveness
   - **Swift/strong**: They will respond quickly and effectively -- proceed with caution
   - **Desperate**: This threatens their core and they will respond irrationally -- expect scorched earth

3. **Trigger points**: What actions would provoke the strongest response? Avoid tripping these unless you are prepared for the fight.

4. **Blind spots**: Based on their assumptions, where are they vulnerable to being surprised? These are your best opportunities.

## 6. SWOT Analysis

Source: Standard framework; guidance from Marcel Planellas, *The Strategic Planning Workbook*

SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) is the most widely used and most widely misused strategy tool. Use it as a synthesis step after completing more rigorous analyses above, not as a standalone exercise.

### How to Use SWOT Correctly

1. Complete the external analysis first (Five Forces, PESTEL, Competitor Analysis). Opportunities and Threats should come from these analyses, not from brainstorming.
2. Complete the internal analysis first (VRIO, Value Chain, EOS Checkup). Strengths and Weaknesses should come from these analyses, not from wishful thinking.
3. List items in each quadrant. Then -- and this is the step most people skip -- cross-reference them to generate strategic options:
   - **S-O strategies**: Use strengths to capture opportunities
   - **W-O strategies**: Address weaknesses to enable opportunities
   - **S-T strategies**: Use strengths to defend against threats
   - **W-T strategies**: Address weaknesses that make threats more dangerous

### Common Misuses to Avoid

- **Brainstorming SWOT without prior analysis.** SWOT generated off the top of your head is just opinions. It reflects biases and recency effects, not strategic reality.
- **Confusing internal and external.** Strengths and Weaknesses are internal (things you control). Opportunities and Threats are external (things happening in the environment). "Our competitors are weak" is NOT a strength -- it is an opportunity.
- **Being vague.** "Good reputation" is useless. "Reputation as the leading competitive intelligence provider in the energy sector, validated by three unsolicited referrals in Q2" is useful.
- **Making it a wish list.** Listing "opportunities" that you have no capability or intent to pursue is noise. Only include opportunities you could realistically act on.
- **Using it as the whole strategy.** SWOT is a summary tool, not a strategy generator. It organizes information for decision-making. The decisions still need to be made using frameworks like Rumelt's Kernel or the Lafley/Martin Cascade.
- **Equal weighting.** Not all items matter equally. After listing, rank by impact. The top item in each quadrant matters more than the bottom five combined.

## 7. Internal Analysis: VRIO Framework

Source: Michael Hitt, R. Duane Ireland, and Robert E. Hoskisson, *Strategic Management*

VRIO evaluates whether CTRS's internal resources and capabilities can be sources of sustained competitive advantage. Apply this to each resource or capability you believe is strategically important.

### The Four Criteria

Evaluate each resource or capability against all four criteria in sequence. The sequence matters -- if a resource fails an earlier test, later tests are irrelevant.

**V -- Valuable**

Does this resource or capability enable CTRS to exploit an opportunity or neutralize a threat in the environment?

Questions:
- Does this help us win clients or retain them?
- Does this improve the quality or efficiency of our work?
- Does this reduce a risk we face?
- If we lost this resource tomorrow, would it hurt?

If NO: This resource is a **competitive disadvantage** if you are investing in it, or irrelevant if you are not. Stop the analysis here.

If YES: Proceed to R.

**R -- Rare**

Is this resource controlled by only a small number of competing firms? If many competitors possess the same valuable resource, it is not a source of advantage -- it is a competitive necessity.

Questions:
- How many competitors have this same capability at a similar level?
- Is this resource widely available in the talent market?
- Could a competitor acquire this resource quickly?

If NO: This resource provides **competitive parity**. You need it to stay in the game, but it won't help you win. Stop the analysis here.

If YES: Proceed to I.

**I -- Inimitable (Costly to Imitate)**

Would it be costly or difficult for competitors to obtain or develop this resource? Three reasons a resource might be hard to imitate:

1. **Unique historical conditions**: The resource was built over time through specific circumstances that cannot be recreated. (e.g., a decade of deep relationships in a specific industry)
2. **Causal ambiguity**: Competitors cannot identify exactly what creates the advantage, so they cannot copy it. (e.g., the combination of culture, methodology, and talent that produces superior analysis)
3. **Social complexity**: The resource is embedded in relationships, culture, or trust that cannot be bought or replicated quickly. (e.g., team dynamics, client trust, reputation)

Questions:
- If a competitor tried to replicate this, how long would it take and what would it cost?
- Is the source of this capability visible and understandable to outsiders?
- Is it embedded in relationships or culture rather than in systems or individuals?

If NO: This resource provides **temporary competitive advantage**. Exploit it now, but expect competitors to catch up. Start building the next advantage.

If YES: Proceed to O.

**O -- Organized**

Is CTRS organized to capture the full value of this resource? Having a valuable, rare, hard-to-copy resource is worthless if the firm's structure, processes, and incentives prevent exploiting it.

Questions:
- Are the right people assigned to leverage this resource?
- Do our processes support and enhance this capability, or do they create friction?
- Do our incentives encourage the behaviors that sustain this advantage?
- Is leadership aware of this advantage and actively protecting it?
- Are we investing in maintaining and deepening this resource?

If NO: You have the potential for **sustained competitive advantage** but are leaving value on the table. Fix the organizational barriers.

If YES: This is a source of **sustained competitive advantage**. Protect it, invest in it, and build your strategy around it.

### VRIO Summary Table

| Resource/Capability | V | R | I | O | Competitive Implication |
|---------------------|---|---|---|---|------------------------|
| Deep expertise in sector X | Yes | Yes | Yes (10-year relationships) | Partially (no succession plan) | Potential sustained advantage -- fix O |
| AI-augmented research tools | Yes | No (competitors have similar) | -- | -- | Competitive parity -- must maintain |
| Speed of delivery | Yes | Yes | No (competitors can hire more people) | -- | Temporary advantage -- exploit now |
| ... | | | | | |

Identify: Which resources are your sustained advantages? Are you organized to capture their full value? Which are temporary -- and what will replace them when competitors catch up?

## 8. Value Chain Analysis

Source: Michael Porter, *Competitive Advantage*; adapted here for a professional services firm.

Porter's original value chain was designed for manufacturing. For CTRS, adapt the categories to reflect how a market intelligence firm creates and delivers value. The goal: identify which activities create the most value for clients and which are sources of cost without corresponding value.

### Primary Activities

These are the activities directly involved in creating and delivering the service to clients.

**1. Business Development / Client Acquisition**

Activities: Lead generation, relationship building, proposal writing, pricing, contract negotiation.

Analyze:
- How do new clients find CTRS? Which channels are most effective?
- What is the cost of acquiring a new client?
- What is the win rate on proposals? What drives wins vs. losses?
- Is business development concentrated in one person (usually the owner)?

**2. Project Scoping and Design**

Activities: Understanding client needs, defining the research question, designing the methodology, scoping the engagement, setting expectations.

Analyze:
- How well does CTRS translate client questions into researchable frameworks?
- How often does scope creep occur? What causes it?
- Is scoping done by senior people or delegated?
- Does CTRS have a repeatable scoping methodology or does every engagement start from scratch?

**3. Research and Analysis (the core production activity)**

Activities: Data collection, source identification, interviews, analysis, synthesis, insight generation.

Analyze:
- What is CTRS's research methodology? Is it documented?
- Where does the most value-added analysis happen?
- Which research tasks are highly skilled vs. routine?
- Where do bottlenecks occur in the research process?
- How is quality controlled? Who reviews analytical work?

**4. Deliverable Creation**

Activities: Report writing, visualization, presentation preparation, dashboard creation.

Analyze:
- How much time is spent on formatting vs. insight generation?
- Are templates and standards in place, or does every deliverable start from scratch?
- What format do clients value most? Is that what CTRS produces?
- Could any deliverable creation be automated or templated?

**5. Client Delivery and Follow-Up**

Activities: Presenting findings, facilitating client discussions, handling questions, ensuring recommendations are actionable, gathering feedback, maintaining the relationship.

Analyze:
- Does CTRS deliver and walk away, or ensure the intelligence is actually used?
- How is client satisfaction measured?
- What drives repeat business?
- Is there a systematic follow-up process?

### Support Activities

These activities support the primary activities and each other.

**Firm Infrastructure**

Leadership, finance, accounting, legal, quality management, strategic planning.
- Is the owner doing everything, or are these functions covered?
- What administrative tasks consume the most time with the least value?

**Human Resource Management**

Recruiting, training, development, compensation, retention.
- How does CTRS attract and retain analytical talent?
- Is there a development path for analysts?
- Is knowledge concentrated in one or two people?

**Technology Development**

Tools, platforms, AI capabilities, research infrastructure.
- What technology does CTRS use for research, analysis, and delivery?
- Is the technology stack providing a competitive edge or just keeping up?
- Where would technology investment create the greatest improvement?

**Procurement**

Data subscriptions, tool licenses, subcontractor relationships.
- What are the key purchased inputs?
- Are there better or cheaper alternatives?
- Are vendor relationships managed proactively or reactively?

### Value Chain Diagnostic Questions

After mapping the value chain:

1. **Where does CTRS create the most value for clients?** (Ask clients, not yourself.) Invest disproportionately here.
2. **Where is CTRS spending time on activities that clients do not value?** Eliminate, automate, or outsource these.
3. **Which activities are bottlenecks?** (Refer to Chain-Link Systems under Rumelt.) Focus improvement on the weakest link.
4. **Where are the handoffs between activities?** Handoffs are where quality and speed degrade. Map them and streamline.
5. **Which activities could be systematized vs. which must remain bespoke?** Systematize everything that can be systematized so that skilled people spend their time on the bespoke, high-value work.

## 9. Reeves' Environment Assessment

Source: Martin Reeves, Knut Haanaes, and Janmejaya Sinha, *Your Strategy Needs a Strategy*

The central insight: there is no single right approach to strategy. The right approach depends on the environment you are operating in. Before choosing a strategy type, assess the environment along three dimensions.

### The Three Dimensions

**Dimension 1: Predictability**

Can you forecast the future of your market with confidence?

Indicators of HIGH predictability:
- Market demand changes slowly and incrementally
- Competitors behave consistently and their strategies are visible
- Technology evolution is gradual, not disruptive
- Client needs change slowly
- Historical trends are reliable guides to the future

Indicators of LOW predictability:
- Market demand shifts rapidly or unexpectedly
- New competitors or business models appear frequently
- Technology is changing what is possible at a fast pace
- Client needs are evolving in unclear directions
- Historical trends are poor guides -- disruption is happening

For CTRS: Assess predictability at the level of your specific market segment, not "consulting" broadly. A segment serving regulated industries (e.g., healthcare compliance intelligence) may be highly predictable. A segment serving tech startups may be highly unpredictable.

**Dimension 2: Malleability**

Can you or your competitors shape the environment -- influence market demand, change industry structure, set standards -- or is the environment an immovable given?

Indicators of HIGH malleability:
- The market is young or fragmented
- No dominant players have set the rules yet
- New business models can redefine what "market intelligence" means
- Clients are open to new ways of buying and consuming intelligence
- Industry standards and norms are still forming

Indicators of LOW malleability:
- The market is mature with established players and norms
- Client procurement processes are rigid
- Industry structure is stable and difficult to influence
- Regulatory frameworks constrain innovation
- "This is how it's always been done" is a common refrain

For CTRS: A small firm has limited ability to shape large, established markets. But within a specific niche, a firm like CTRS may have significant shaping power -- especially if the niche is emerging and norms have not yet solidified.

**Dimension 3: Harshness**

Is your firm's survival at stake? Harshness exists when the environment is actively hostile -- declining demand, price wars, resource scarcity, or existential threats.

Indicators of HIGH harshness:
- Revenue is declining or at risk of sudden loss
- A major client loss would threaten the firm's viability
- Cash reserves are thin (less than 3 months of operating expenses)
- Market demand for your services is shrinking
- A disruptive force (AI, new competitor, regulation) threatens to eliminate your offering
- The firm is in a turnaround or crisis situation

Indicators of LOW harshness:
- Revenue is stable or growing
- Client base is diversified
- Cash reserves provide a comfortable buffer
- Demand for your services is strong
- No existential threats on the horizon

For CTRS: Be honest about harshness. Small firms often exist closer to the harsh end than their owners admit. If losing one client would put the firm at risk, the environment is harsh regardless of how the broader market looks.

### The Five Strategy Types

Based on the three dimensions, identify which strategy approach is appropriate. Do not force-fit CTRS into a strategy type that feels aspirational -- match to reality.

**1. Classical Strategy**

*Environment: Predictable, Not Malleable, Not Harsh*

The environment is stable and you cannot change it, but you are not in danger. Compete on position and scale.

Approach:
- Analyze the market structure thoroughly (Five Forces, Competitor Analysis)
- Build a sustainable competitive position (Lafley/Martin Cascade)
- Optimize operations for efficiency
- Plan in detail and execute the plan

Indicators you are in a Classical environment:
- You can confidently forecast next year's revenue within 15%
- Your competitors have been the same firms for years
- Client needs are well-understood and stable
- The winning formula is clear -- the question is execution

**2. Adaptive Strategy**

*Environment: Unpredictable, Not Malleable, Not Harsh*

You cannot predict what will happen and you cannot shape the market, but you are not in crisis. Compete on speed and flexibility.

Approach:
- Run many small experiments rather than making big bets
- Build a portfolio of strategic options
- Develop rapid feedback loops to learn what works
- Iterate constantly -- plan in short cycles (weeks, not years)
- Maintain organizational flexibility (avoid lock-in)

Indicators you are in an Adaptive environment:
- Your forecasts are frequently wrong
- New competitors or substitute offerings appear regularly
- Client needs shift in ways you did not anticipate
- What worked last quarter may not work next quarter
- The firms that win are the ones that adapt fastest, not the ones with the best plans

**3. Visionary Strategy**

*Environment: Predictable, Malleable, Not Harsh*

You can see the future and you can shape it. Compete by being first to create and capture a new market.

Approach:
- Define a bold vision for how the market should work
- Build the offering before demand is proven
- Invest ahead of the curve
- Persuade clients and the market to follow your vision
- Move fast to establish position before others see the opportunity

Indicators you are in a Visionary environment:
- You can see a clear unmet need that others have not addressed
- You have the capability to create something genuinely new
- Clients are receptive to new approaches when shown them
- The timing is right -- the enabling conditions are in place
- First-mover advantage is real in this space

**4. Shaping Strategy**

*Environment: Unpredictable, Malleable, Not Harsh*

The future is uncertain but you can influence how it unfolds. Compete by orchestrating an ecosystem.

Approach:
- Build a platform or ecosystem, not just a product
- Collaborate with partners, even competitors, to shape market norms
- Engage stakeholders early to co-create the direction
- Be flexible on the details but persistent on the direction
- Use influence, not control

Indicators you are in a Shaping environment:
- The market is new or being redefined
- Multiple possible futures exist and no one has won yet
- Partnerships and alliances can influence the outcome
- Standards and norms are still being established
- Your firm's actions, combined with others, can tip the market in a favorable direction

**5. Renewal Strategy**

*Environment: Harsh (regardless of predictability or malleability)*

Survival is the immediate concern. Compete by restoring viability first, then choosing a new strategy type.

Approach:
- **Phase 1 -- Survive**: Cut costs, preserve cash, stabilize revenue, address the immediate threat. Do not invest in growth until the foundation is secure.
- **Phase 2 -- Pivot**: Once survival is secured, assess the environment and choose one of the four strategies above. The renewed firm may need a different strategy than the pre-crisis firm.

Indicators you are in a Renewal environment:
- Revenue has declined significantly or a major revenue source is at imminent risk
- Cash reserves are dangerously low
- The current business model is broken or the market has shifted away from you
- Internal dysfunction (key departures, morale collapse, operational failures) is compounding external threats
- Continuing the current strategy will lead to failure

**Critical rule**: If the environment is harsh, you are in Renewal regardless of predictability or malleability. Address harshness first. Only after the firm is stabilized should you assess the other two dimensions and choose a long-term strategy type.

Complete this assessment at the start of each quarterly planning session:

|-----------|--------------|-------------|

## 10. Using These Frameworks Together

Do not run every framework every time. Match the analysis to the question at hand. Here is the recommended sequence for a full quarterly diagnostic:

1. **Start with Rumelt's "What's going on here?"** -- Get the lay of the land before applying structured frameworks.
2. **PESTEL scan** (15-20 min) -- Identify any major external shifts since last quarter.
3. **Reeves' Environment Assessment** (10 min) -- Confirm you are using the right strategy type.
4. **EOS Organizational Checkup** (20 min) -- Assess internal health across all six components.
5. **Five Forces** (only if the competitive landscape has changed or you are evaluating a new market).
6. **Competitor Analysis** (only for specific competitors where you need to predict behavior).
7. **VRIO** (only if you need to evaluate whether a specific capability is a real advantage).
8. **Value Chain** (only if you suspect internal inefficiency or want to identify where to invest).
9. **SWOT synthesis** -- Summarize findings from the above into a cross-referenced SWOT.
10. **Rumelt's Diagnosis statement** -- Synthesize everything into one paragraph naming the core challenge.

For a routine quarterly session where nothing dramatic has changed, steps 1, 2, 3, 4, and 10 are sufficient. Add others as needed.
