## New Strategic Question: Analyst Firm Partnership

### The Situation

TechVantage Research (a mid-tier analyst firm, ~80 analysts, primarily covering enterprise software) approached Sarah last week about a "strategic partnership." Their proposal: Apex would become their preferred subcontractor for competitive intelligence projects in the B2B SaaS space. They'd send us 4-6 engagements per quarter at a fixed rate, and we'd deliver under the TechVantage brand. They framed it as "guaranteed revenue and instant scale."

### Game Theory Assessment

**What's their incentive?** TechVantage is likely responding to the same Forrester/NarrowBridge acquisition that we noted in our Q3 planning. They need a CI capability to stay competitive but don't want to build one internally (expensive, slow). They'd rather rent ours. The fixed rate they proposed ($12K per engagement) is below our average ($18K), meaning they'd resell our work at significant margin. Their incentive is to lock us in cheaply before we realize our leverage.

**What's the larger game?** If we become dependent on TechVantage for 30-40% of our pipeline, they have pricing power over us. They could squeeze rates after Year 1 once we've built our operations around their volume. They also control the client relationship -- we'd never meet the end client, couldn't convert them to direct relationships, and would have no brand visibility. This is a classic outsourcing relationship that benefits the platform, not the supplier.

**What would they do if we said no?** They'd approach another boutique. There are 3-4 firms they could call. This tells us we're not their only option and we shouldn't negotiate from a position of scarcity.

### Reverse Engineering: What Would Have to Be True for This to Be a Good Deal?

| Condition | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| The fixed rate would need to be at or above our target margin | No. $12K/engagement is 33% below our average. Even accounting for lower sales cost, margin would be thin. |
| The volume would need to fill genuine idle capacity | Partially. We do have capacity gaps between engagements. But with Jenna starting, we expect those gaps to shrink. |
| We'd need to retain the right to build direct relationships with end clients | Their proposal explicitly prohibits this. Non-negotiable for them. |
| The partnership would need to strengthen our brand, not dilute it | No. White-label work is invisible work. It doesn't help us build the reputation we need. |
| We'd need an exit clause that lets us walk away without revenue disruption | Their proposed terms include a 12-month exclusivity clause in B2B SaaS CI. This would prevent us from working with competing analyst firms AND potentially conflict with our direct client work. |

### Decision

**Decline the partnership as proposed.** The economics are unfavorable, the exclusivity clause is dangerous, and white-label work directly contradicts our strategy of building brand recognition as the specialist in B2B SaaS competitive intelligence.

**Counter-offer worth exploring:** Propose a referral arrangement instead. TechVantage refers clients to us for CI projects, we deliver under our own brand, and TechVantage receives a 15% referral fee. This preserves our client relationships, brand visibility, and pricing power. If they say no, that confirms their goal was margin capture, not genuine partnership.

Sarah will respond to TechVantage with the counter-proposal by August 14.
