## 1. EOS (Entrepreneurial Operating System)

Source: Gino Wickman, *Traction*.

EOS is a complete operating system for running a small to mid-size business. It provides a set of simple, practical tools that, used together, create a self-reinforcing system for getting what you want from your business. For CTRS, EOS is the backbone execution framework -- the default system for translating quarterly strategic decisions into daily action.

### The Six Key Components

Every business has six key components. Strengthen all six and the business runs well. Neglect any one and the whole system suffers. Rate each 1-10 to diagnose where CTRS is weakest.

**1. Vision** -- Does everyone in the company share the same vision and are they rowing in the same direction?
- Test: Ask each team member separately where CTRS will be in three years. If answers diverge, Vision is weak.
- Tool: The V/TO (see below).

**2. People** -- Do you have the right people in the right seats?
- Test: Does every person share your core values AND do they get their role, want it, and have the capacity to do it?
- Tool: The People Analyzer and Accountability Chart (see below).

**3. Data** -- Do you have a handful of objective numbers that give you a pulse on the business weekly?
- Test: Can you tell by Tuesday whether last week was a good week, without relying on gut feeling or financials?
- Tool: The Scorecard (see below).

**4. Issues** -- Do you identify issues openly and solve them permanently?
- Test: Do the same problems recur? Do people avoid raising hard topics?
- Tool: The Issues List and IDS process (see below).

**5. Process** -- Have you documented and simplified your core processes so they're followed consistently?
- Test: Would a new hire know exactly how CTRS delivers an engagement from sale to completion?
- Tool: The Three-Step Process Documenter (see below).

**6. Traction** -- Do you execute on your vision with discipline and accountability every week?
- Test: Do you set quarterly priorities and hit at least 80% of them?
- Tool: Rocks, the Level 10 Meeting, and the Scorecard (see below).

### The V/TO (Vision/Traction Organizer)

The V/TO is a two-page document that captures the company's complete vision and plan. It answers eight questions. Complete these in order, top to bottom. Revisit at least annually; refresh the bottom sections quarterly.

#### Question 1: What are your Core Values?

Core values are 3-7 defining characteristics of the organization. They are discovered, not invented -- they already exist in your best people.

**How to define them:**
1. List the names of 3-5 people who, if you could clone them, would make the company unstoppable.
2. For each person, list the characteristics that make them great.
3. Look for patterns. Circle the traits that appear for multiple people.
4. Consolidate into 3-7 core values. Use plain language, not corporate jargon.
5. Test: Would you fire a high performer who consistently violated one of these values? If not, it's not a true core value.

**For CTRS, ask:** What characteristics define the people who do the best work for our clients? What behaviors are non-negotiable regardless of how much revenue someone generates?

#### Question 2: What is your Core Focus?

Two parts:
- **Purpose/Cause/Passion:** Why does CTRS exist beyond making money? What drives the work? Complete the sentence: "We exist to ___."
- **Niche:** What is CTRS uniquely designed to do better than anyone? This is the one thing you do best. Not a list -- one thing.

**Test:** Every new opportunity should pass through the Core Focus filter. If a project doesn't align with your niche, say no regardless of how profitable it looks.

#### Question 3: What is your 10-Year Target?

One big, audacious goal. This is what Collins calls a BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal). Make it specific enough to know unambiguously when you've hit it.

**Rules:**
- One goal, not a list.
- 10-25 years out.
- Specific and measurable.
- Energizing -- it should make you slightly nervous and very excited.

**Examples for a firm like CTRS:** "Become the go-to market intelligence partner for the top 50 [specific industry] firms in North America." Or: "Build a $10M revenue intelligence practice with a proprietary methodology used by 200+ companies."

#### Question 4: What is your Marketing Strategy?

Four components:
- **Target market:** Describe your ideal client with enough specificity that any team member could identify one. Demographics, firmographics, psychographics. The "list" of companies or types you go after.
- **Three Uniques:** Three things that, taken together, differentiate CTRS from every competitor. No competitor should be able to claim all three simultaneously.
- **Proven process:** A visual, named, and documented high-level process that explains how CTRS delivers value. 3-7 steps. Name it something proprietary.
- **Guarantee:** An explicit or implicit promise to clients. What do you stand behind? This doesn't have to be a money-back guarantee -- it can be a service promise or performance commitment.

#### Question 5: What is your Three-Year Picture?

Describe what CTRS looks like in three years if the strategy works. Be concrete:
- Revenue and profit targets.
- Number of clients and client types.
- Number of team members.
- What services you offer (and which you've stopped).
- What the physical or operational setup looks like.
- What you're known for in the market.

**Write it in present tense as if you're already there.** "It is [date three years from now]. CTRS has ___."

#### Question 6: What is your One-Year Plan?

The most important number: revenue target for the year. Then add:
- Profit target.
- 3-7 specific goals for the year. These are the boulders you must move this year to stay on track for the Three-Year Picture.

**Each goal must be SMART:** Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, Timely.

#### Question 7: What are your Quarterly Rocks?

See the full Rocks section below. The V/TO captures:
- 3-7 company Rocks for the current quarter.
- Each Rock has one owner, even if multiple people contribute.
- Each Rock is specific, measurable, and has a deadline (the end of the quarter).

#### Question 8: What are your Issues?

The Issues List -- a master list of every obstacle, idea, concern, and opportunity that needs to be addressed. This list is never empty. It gets longer before it gets shorter. That's healthy.

**Three categories of issues:**
1. Issues that must be solved this quarter (move to the weekly Level 10 agenda).
2. Issues that belong on the long-term list (park them for a future quarterly session).
3. Issues that belong on someone's departmental list.

**Rule:** Never try to solve issues in the moment they're raised. Capture them, then use IDS in the proper forum.

### The People Analyzer

Use this tool to evaluate whether each person at CTRS is the right person in the right seat.

#### Part 1: Core Values Evaluation

Create a grid. Names down the left column, core values across the top. Rate each person on each value:

- **+** = Most of the time they exhibit this value.
- **+/-** = Sometimes they do, sometimes they don't.
- **-** = Most of the time they don't exhibit this value.

**The bar:** Decide the minimum standard. Typical bar: no minuses, and no more than one +/-. Anyone below the bar is the wrong person for CTRS regardless of their skills.

**How to use the results:**
- All pluses: Right person. Invest in them.
- One +/- and rest pluses: Right person. Have a direct conversation about the one value. Give 30-90 days.
- Two or more +/- or any minus: Wrong person. Begin a candid conversation about fit. If no improvement in 30 days, make the change.

#### Part 2: GWC (Get It, Want It, Capacity to Do It)

For each person in their specific seat, answer three questions:

- **Get It?** Do they truly understand the role -- its responsibilities, its challenges, how it connects to the company? Instinctive, not trainable.
- **Want It?** Do they genuinely want this role? Not as a stepping stone, not as an obligation -- they want it. Only they can answer this honestly.
- **Capacity to Do It?** Do they have the intellectual, physical, emotional, time, and knowledge capacity to do the job well? This one can sometimes be developed.

**All three must be yes.** If any one is "no," the person is in the wrong seat. Rearrange the chart before defaulting to termination -- sometimes a great person is in the wrong seat.

**CTRS Application:** In a 2-10 person firm, every wrong-seat situation is magnified. One person in the wrong seat can drag down 10-50% of the company. Address People issues faster than you think you should.

### The Accountability Chart

The Accountability Chart replaces the traditional org chart. It starts with the structure of the business, not the people currently filling roles.

**Rules:**
1. **Design the structure first, then fill it with people.** Never create a role because you have a person. Create roles because the business needs them.
2. **Every function has one and only one owner.** Shared accountability is no accountability.
3. **Every seat has 5 roles (major responsibilities).** Not tasks -- roles. If a seat has more than 5 roles, it's overloaded and needs to be split.
4. **One person can sit in multiple seats** (common in small firms), but each seat is still defined separately so you know when it's time to hire.
5. **No dotted lines.** Every person reports to exactly one person.

**Structure for CTRS at its current size:**

Start with three core functions that exist in every business:
- **Sales/Marketing** -- generating and closing new business.
- **Operations** -- delivering the work (for CTRS: running market intelligence engagements).
- **Finance/Admin** -- managing the money, HR, legal, admin.

Above these sits the **Integrator** (the person who runs the day-to-day, harmonizes the functions, resolves cross-functional issues) and optionally a **Visionary** (the person who sets the direction, manages big relationships, generates new ideas).

**In a small firm, the owner is often both Visionary and Integrator.** That's fine temporarily, but recognize these are fundamentally different roles. As CTRS grows, separating them is one of the highest-leverage hires you can make.

**For each seat, define:**
1. The name of the seat (role title).
2. The 5 key roles (major responsibilities).
3. The person who fills the seat.
4. The GWC evaluation for that person in that seat.

### Rocks (90-Day Priorities)

Rocks are the 3-7 most important things that must be accomplished in the next 90 days. The term comes from Stephen Covey's analogy: if you fill a jar with sand (small tasks) first, the big rocks won't fit. Put the big rocks in first.

#### How to Set Rocks

**Step 1: Review the V/TO.** Look at the One-Year Plan goals, the Three-Year Picture, and the Issues List. Rocks should advance these.

**Step 2: Brainstorm.** Each team member lists everything they think must happen this quarter. No filtering yet.

**Step 3: Narrow the company list to 3-7.** More than 7 is too many -- you'll dilute focus. Force prioritization. Ask: "If we could only accomplish 3 things this quarter, which 3 would move the needle most?"

**Step 4: Assign one owner per Rock.** The owner is accountable for the outcome, even if others help execute. Never assign a Rock to two people.

**Step 5: Make each Rock SMART.**
- **Specific:** What exactly will be done? "Improve marketing" is not a Rock. "Launch a monthly market intelligence newsletter targeting CFOs in mid-market SaaS companies" is.
- **Measurable:** How will you know it's done? Define a binary completion criterion.
- **Attainable:** Can this realistically be done in 90 days with the resources available?
- **Realistic:** Does this matter enough to be one of the 3-7 most important things?
- **Timely:** Deadline is end of quarter. No extensions.

**Step 6: Set individual Rocks.** Each person gets 3-7 personal Rocks. These should include their share of company Rocks plus any role-specific priorities.

#### Rock Review Process

- **Weekly:** In the Level 10 Meeting, each Rock is reported as "on track" or "off track." No lengthy discussion -- just the status. Off-track Rocks drop to the Issues List for IDS.
- **End of quarter:** Each Rock is scored as "done" or "not done." No partial credit. Target: 80% or more completion across all Rocks.
- **If consistently below 80%:** You're setting too many Rocks, setting them too ambitiously, or not protecting Rock time in the weekly schedule.
- **If consistently at 100%:** You're not being ambitious enough. Raise the bar.

#### CTRS Rock Examples

- "Close 2 new retainer clients in [target vertical] with minimum $5K/month engagement by [end of quarter]."
- "Document the core delivery process (from client onboarding to final deliverable) with checklists for each stage by [end of quarter]."
- "Hire and onboard a research analyst who meets all core values and GWC criteria by [end of quarter]."
- "Build a lead generation pipeline that produces 10 qualified conversations per month by [end of quarter]."
- "Reduce owner involvement in active engagement delivery to under 30% of weekly hours by [end of quarter]."

### The Level 10 Meeting

The Level 10 Meeting is a weekly 90-minute meeting with a fixed agenda. It's called "Level 10" because the target is for every participant to rate the meeting a 10 out of 10 for productivity. Same day, same time, every week. Never cancel it.

#### Exact Agenda with Time Allocations

| Segment | Time | What Happens |
|---------|------|-------------|
| **Segue** | 5 min | Each person shares one good thing -- personal or professional -- from the past week. Sets human tone. |
| **Scorecard Review** | 5 min | Review the weekly Scorecard numbers. Only discuss numbers that are off track. Off-track numbers drop to the Issues List. No rabbit holes. |
| **Rock Review** | 5 min | Each Rock: on track or off track. That's it. Off-track Rocks drop to the Issues List. |
| **Customer/Employee Headlines** | 5 min | Any notable news about clients or team members. Good news or bad. Quick headlines only -- details go to Issues List. |
| **To-Do List Review** | 5 min | Review last week's to-dos. Each is reported as "done" or "not done." Target: 90%+ completion. To-dos are 7-day action items, distinct from 90-day Rocks. |
| **IDS (Issues Solving)** | 60 min | This is where the real work happens. Prioritize the Issues List top-3. Use the IDS process (see below) for each. Generate to-dos. Repeat until time runs out. |
| **Conclude** | 5 min | Recap the to-do list. Identify any cascading messages (what needs to be communicated to people not in the room). Rate the meeting 1-10. |

**Rules:**
- Start on time. End on time. No exceptions.
- No phones or laptops for email. Full attention.
- Every issue that surfaces goes on the Issues List -- never solve it inline if it's not in the IDS segment.
- Meeting rating must average 8+ consistently. If it drops, diagnose why and fix the meeting before the next week.
- In a small firm like CTRS with 2-5 people, every team member attends. As the team grows past 6-7, split into a leadership team meeting and department meetings with the same format.

### The Issues Solving Track (IDS)

IDS stands for **Identify, Discuss, Solve.** It is the one process you must master. Most teams spend 80% of their time discussing and 20% solving. IDS flips this.

#### Step 1: Identify

State the real issue. Not the symptom -- the root cause. Most listed issues are symptoms. Keep asking "What's the real issue here?" or "Why is this happening?" until you hit the root.

**Rules for Identify:**
- One person states the issue clearly in 1-2 sentences.
- The group confirms: "Is this the real issue or a symptom of something deeper?"
- Restate the issue if needed. Don't move forward until the root cause is named.

**Example:** The issue listed is "We missed the deadline on the Henderson project." The root cause might be: "We don't have a project management system that tracks deadlines and workload across engagements."

#### Step 2: Discuss

Brief, focused conversation about solutions. Not about the problem -- about solutions.

**Rules for Discuss:**
- No repeating. Once a point is made, don't make it again.
- No tangents. If someone raises a different issue, capture it on the Issues List and return to the current one.
- No politics. Say what needs to be said.
- Three to five minutes is usually enough. The discussion should be shorter than you think.

#### Step 3: Solve

End with a clear action. Every solved issue produces one of three outcomes:
1. **A to-do** -- a 7-day action item assigned to one person.
2. **A change** -- a policy, process, or structural change that is decided and documented right now.
3. **A killed issue** -- the group decides it's not worth solving and removes it from the list permanently.

**Rules for Solve:**
- The solution must have an owner.
- If it's a to-do, it goes on the To-Do List for next week's review.
- If the issue will take longer than a week, break it into a 7-day first step and put the larger project on the Issues List or make it a Rock.
- Once solved, cross it off the Issues List. Do not revisit it.

**Common IDS Mistakes:**
- Discussing too long without converging on a solution.
- Solving symptoms instead of root causes (the issue keeps reappearing).
- Not assigning a clear owner.
- Being polite instead of honest during Discuss.

### The Scorecard

The Scorecard is a weekly report of 5-15 numbers that tell you the pulse of the business. It replaces gut-feel management with objective data.

**Design Principles:**
- Each number has an owner (one person accountable for that metric).
- Each number has a weekly target or acceptable range.
- Numbers that miss their target are flagged and automatically drop to the Issues List.
- The Scorecard should predict problems 2-6 weeks before they hit the financials.
- If you can't tell whether it was a good week by looking at the Scorecard, you're tracking the wrong numbers.

**Categories for CTRS:**

| Category | Example Metrics | Why It Matters |
|----------|----------------|---------------|
| **Revenue Pipeline** | Number of active proposals; value of pipeline; new leads this week | Predicts future revenue 30-90 days out |
| **Client Delivery** | Engagements on schedule (%); deliverables completed this week | Catches delivery problems before clients complain |
| **Utilization** | Billable hours as % of available hours; hours by team member | Core profitability driver for a services firm |
| **Client Satisfaction** | NPS or satisfaction check-ins; renewals/extensions | Leading indicator of retention and referrals |
| **Financial** | Cash position; AR aging; weekly revenue recognized | Ensures the business stays healthy |
| **Business Development** | Outreach activities (emails, calls, meetings); content published | Prevents the feast-famine cycle |

**Rules:**
- Start with 5 numbers. Add more only as the team can handle tracking them consistently.
- If a number is consistently on target for 13+ weeks, consider replacing it with something more useful.
- If a number is consistently off target for 13+ weeks, either fix the underlying problem or reset the target.
- The Scorecard is reviewed in the Level 10 Meeting in 5 minutes or less. If it takes longer, you have too many numbers or you're discussing instead of flagging.

### The Three-Step Process Documenter

Most process documentation fails because it's too detailed and no one reads it. EOS simplifies this to three steps.

**Step 1: Identify your core processes.** List the handful (typically 6-10) of major processes that run the business. For CTRS, these likely include:
- Sales/new client acquisition process.
- Client onboarding process.
- Engagement delivery/research methodology.
- Quality review process.
- Invoicing and collections process.
- Hiring process.
- Content/thought leadership process.

**Step 2: Document each process at a high level.** For each core process, capture 20% of the detail that produces 80% of the results. This means:
- Give the process a name (preferably a proprietary name).
- List the major steps (typically 5-15 per process).
- Under each step, list the key actions or guidelines -- not every micro-task, just the essential ones.
- The whole process should fit on 1-3 pages.

**Step 3: Ensure everyone follows the process consistently.** This is the hardest step. Train each team member on every process relevant to their seat. Manage through the Scorecard and Level 10 Meeting when processes aren't followed.

**CTRS Application:** For a market intelligence firm, the engagement delivery process is the crown jewel. Document how CTRS takes a client from "signed proposal" to "final deliverable and follow-up." This is also your Proven Process for marketing purposes (V/TO Question 4).

### Five Leadership Abilities

These are the five abilities every leader in an EOS-run company must develop. Assess yourself on each.

**1. Simplify**
- Eliminate complexity in everything: communication, processes, structure, offerings.
- Ask: "Can I explain this to a new hire in under two minutes?"
- For CTRS: Is your service offering simple enough that a prospect immediately understands what you do and why it matters? If it takes a 30-minute conversation to explain your value, simplify.

**2. Delegate and Elevate**
- List everything you do. Sort into four quadrants: (a) Love and excel at, (b) Like and are good at, (c) Don't like but are good at, (d) Don't like and aren't good at.
- Delegate everything in (c) and (d) immediately. Over time, delegate (b) as well.
- Elevate yourself to spend 80%+ of your time in quadrant (a).
- For CTRS: The owner likely needs to delegate engagement delivery and admin to spend more time on strategic relationships and business development.

**3. Predict**
- Maintain a short-term (90-day Rocks) and long-term (3-year picture, 10-year target) view simultaneously.
- Build systems that surface problems early (Scorecard, Level 10 issues).
- Develop the ability to see around corners: what will clients need in 18 months? What changes in your market are inevitable?

**4. Systemize**
- Document and standardize core processes (the Three-Step Process Documenter above).
- Build systems so the business isn't dependent on any one person's heroics.
- The test: Could CTRS run for 30 days without the owner making any decisions? If not, what systems are missing?

**5. Structure**
- Maintain the right organizational structure using the Accountability Chart.
- Ensure every person is in the right seat, every function has an owner, and the structure matches where the business is headed -- not just where it is.
- Restructure proactively as the company grows. Don't wait until the current structure breaks.

## 2. Balanced Scorecard

Source: Kaplan & Norton.

The Balanced Scorecard ensures strategy is measured from four perspectives, not just financial. It prevents the common failure mode of a small firm that watches revenue and profit but ignores the drivers underneath.

### The Four Perspectives

#### Perspective 1: Financial

**Question:** To succeed financially, what must we achieve?

For CTRS, set objectives and measures like:

| Objective | Measure | Target |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Grow revenue | Monthly recurring revenue; total quarterly revenue | Set specific targets |
| Improve profitability | Gross margin per engagement; operating margin | Minimum 40% gross, 20% operating |
| Reduce revenue concentration | Revenue from top client as % of total | Below 25% |
| Increase predictable revenue | % of revenue from retainers vs. project work | Growing quarter over quarter |

#### Perspective 2: Customer

**Question:** To achieve our vision, how must we appear to our clients?

| Objective | Measure | Target |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Deliver exceptional insights | Client satisfaction scores; repeat engagement rate | 90%+ satisfaction; 70%+ repeat rate |
| Be the trusted advisor | % of clients using CTRS for strategic decisions (not just data) | Track and grow |
| Win through speed | Average turnaround time vs. client expectation | Consistently early |
| Earn referrals | Number of referral-sourced leads per quarter | Set a floor and grow |

#### Perspective 3: Internal Process

**Question:** To satisfy clients and financial goals, what business processes must we excel at?

| Objective | Measure | Target |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Standardize delivery | % of engagements following the documented process | 100% |
| Improve research efficiency | Hours per engagement type; tool utilization rate | Decreasing trend |
| Strengthen quality control | Number of client-reported errors or rework events | Zero or near-zero |
| Build a sales pipeline machine | Lead-to-proposal conversion rate; proposal-to-close rate | Track and improve |

#### Perspective 4: Learning and Growth

**Question:** To achieve our vision, how must the organization learn and improve?

| Objective | Measure | Target |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Develop team capabilities | Training hours per person per quarter; new skills acquired | Minimum threshold per person |
| Build proprietary methodologies | Number of documented, proprietary frameworks or tools | Growing |
| Invest in technology and tools | Time saved per engagement from tool improvements | Measurable improvement |
| Strengthen culture | Team member satisfaction/engagement; voluntary turnover | High satisfaction; low turnover |

### The Strategic Management System

The Balanced Scorecard operates through four management processes that link long-term strategy to short-term action:

**Process 1: Translating the vision.** Convert the V/TO and strategic direction into specific objectives and measures across all four perspectives. Make strategy concrete enough that every team member can see how their work connects.

**Process 2: Communicating and linking.** Share the scorecard with the entire team. Align individual goals (Rocks, role expectations) to scorecard objectives. Every person should be able to answer: "How does my work this week move a Scorecard measure?"

**Process 3: Business planning.** Use the Scorecard to set quarterly priorities (Rocks). Allocate time, budget, and attention based on which Scorecard measures are furthest from target.

**Process 4: Feedback and learning.** Review the Scorecard monthly. Not just "did we hit the number?" but "is the strategy working?" If measures improve but clients are unhappy, the measures are wrong. If the team is learning but processes aren't improving, there's a transfer problem. Use the Scorecard as a hypothesis about what drives success, and revise the hypotheses as you learn.

**CTRS Integration:** The Balanced Scorecard and the EOS Scorecard are complementary. Use the EOS weekly Scorecard for operational pulse (5-15 numbers reviewed in Level 10 meetings). Use the Balanced Scorecard as a quarterly strategic lens to ensure you're measuring what matters across all four perspectives. The quarterly planning session is where you reconcile the two.

## 3. Hoffman & Finkel's Scaling Framework

Source: David Finkel, *The Leader's Guide to Scaling* (and Maui Mastermind methodology).

This framework is specifically designed for owner-dependent small businesses that want to scale beyond the owner's capacity. It is directly relevant to CTRS's growth trajectory.

### The Level Three Road Map

Businesses evolve through five stages. Each stage has distinct characteristics and challenges. Identify where CTRS is right now.

**Stage 1: Owner-Reliant (Revenue < ~$250K)**
- The owner does most or all of the work.
- No employees, or employees who can only execute specific tasks under direct supervision.
- The business is the owner.
- **Key challenge:** You're selling hours, and you've hit a ceiling.

**Stage 2: Owner-Managed (Revenue ~$250K-$750K)**
- The owner has a small team but manages everything.
- The owner is the bottleneck for decisions, quality control, client relationships, and sales.
- Some processes exist but live in the owner's head.
- **Key challenge:** The owner works harder as the business grows. Growth means more stress, not more freedom.

**Stage 3: Owner-Led (Revenue ~$750K-$2M)**
- The business has systems and a team that can execute without the owner in every conversation.
- The owner focuses on strategy, key relationships, and team development.
- The business can run for 1-4 weeks without the owner and not lose quality.
- **Key challenge:** Building the management layer and letting go of control.

**Stage 4: Professional Management (Revenue ~$2M-$5M)**
- A management team runs day-to-day operations.
- The owner focuses on vision, culture, and strategic moves.
- Systems and controls are mature enough that the business grows predictably.
- **Key challenge:** Maintaining culture and quality while the owner steps further back.

**Stage 5: Scalable Enterprise (Revenue > $5M)**
- The business can grow significantly without proportional increases in management complexity.
- Multiple service lines, teams, or markets running on proven systems.
- **Key challenge:** Avoiding bureaucracy and maintaining innovation.

### The Self-Employment Trap

The Self-Employment Trap is the single most important concept for CTRS to understand. It describes what happens when an owner who is good at their craft starts a business.

**The trap works like this:**
1. The owner is skilled at doing the work (market intelligence research, analysis, client delivery).
2. The business gets clients because the owner is good.
3. As the business grows, the owner spends more time managing and selling and less time doing.
4. Quality dips because the owner can't be everywhere. Or the owner refuses to delegate and burns out.
5. Growth stalls because the owner is the constraint. Every hour of growth requires more of the owner's time.

**The escape:**
- Stop being the best practitioner and start being the best builder of a practitioner team.
- Document your methodology so others can execute it (Three-Step Process Documenter).
- Hire for capability, not just task completion.
- Accept that others will do it differently (perhaps 80% as well at first), and that this is the cost of scaling.
- Measure results, not activity. If the client deliverable meets the quality bar, it doesn't matter that the analyst used a different research approach than you would have.

**CTRS Test:** Calculate how many hours per week the owner spends on client delivery vs. business building (sales, systems, team development, strategy). If delivery is more than 50% of the owner's time, you are in the Self-Employment Trap.

### The Three-Legged Stool

A business that can scale rests on three legs. If any leg is weak, the stool tips over. Assess each leg for CTRS.

#### Leg 1: Systems

Systems are the documented, repeatable processes that allow the business to operate without depending on any one person's memory or judgment.

**Subtypes:**
- **Delivery systems:** How CTRS executes an engagement from start to finish. Documented methodology, templates, quality checklists.
- **Sales systems:** How leads are generated, qualified, proposed to, and closed. Pipeline stages, follow-up cadences, proposal templates.
- **Administrative systems:** How invoicing, contracts, scheduling, and internal communication work.
- **Quality control systems:** How deliverables are reviewed before going to clients. Who checks what, using what criteria.

**Assessment questions:**
- Could a new hire execute an engagement to your quality standard using only the written documentation?
- Is your sales process documented well enough that someone else could run it?
- Do you have templates for every recurring deliverable?

#### Leg 2: Team

The team is the people who execute within the systems.

**Subtypes:**
- **A-players:** People who share your core values AND excel in their seat. These are who you hire, develop, and retain.
- **Managers (as you grow):** People who can lead, train, and hold others accountable -- not just do the work themselves.
- **Specialists vs. generalists:** In a small firm, you need generalists who can wear multiple hats. As you scale, you replace them with specialists.
- **Culture carriers:** People who embody and enforce the core values, especially when the owner isn't in the room.

**Assessment questions:**
- Do you have a documented hiring process that screens for core values and GWC?
- Can any team member handle a client relationship without the owner's involvement?
- Is there anyone whose departure would cripple the business? (That's a risk, not a strength.)

#### Leg 3: Controls

Controls are the mechanisms that keep the business on track.

**Subtypes:**
- **Financial controls:** Budget, cash flow management, pricing discipline, expense approval thresholds.
- **Quality controls:** Deliverable review processes, client feedback loops, error tracking.
- **Operational controls:** Scorecard metrics, project tracking, capacity management.
- **Strategic controls:** Quarterly planning sessions, Rock reviews, annual V/TO refresh.

**Assessment questions:**
- Do you know your profitability by engagement type and by client?
- Do you have a dashboard you check weekly that tells you the health of the business?
- When something goes wrong, is it caught by a system or discovered by accident?

### The Five Pillars of Business

Every business must be strong in five pillars. Rate CTRS 1-10 on each:

1. **Sales and Marketing:** Can CTRS reliably generate leads and close business? Is there a pipeline or is it feast-and-famine?
2. **Operations:** Can CTRS deliver consistently at high quality without the owner in every engagement?
3. **Finance:** Does CTRS manage cash, pricing, and profitability proactively? Are financial decisions based on data?
4. **Team:** Does CTRS have the right people, properly trained, with clear accountability?
5. **Executive Leadership:** Is the owner spending time on the right things? Are they leading the business or running it?

The weakest pillar caps the business. Find it and fix it before trying to grow.

### The "Hit by a Bus" Test

Ask: "If the owner were unable to work for 90 days starting tomorrow, what would happen?"

**Run the test:**
1. List every function the owner performs (client work, sales, invoicing, hiring, quality review, strategy, etc.).
2. For each function, answer: Who would do this? Do they know how? Is it documented?
3. Any function with "nobody" or "they'd figure it out" is a critical vulnerability.
4. Prioritize building redundancy for the highest-risk functions first.

**For CTRS:** The most common vulnerabilities are:
- Client relationships that only the owner holds.
- The sales process (owner is the only one who sells).
- Quality review (owner is the only one who checks work before it goes to clients).
- Institutional knowledge about methodologies, past research, and client history.

### The 90-Day Strategic Plan Process

Hoffman & Finkel's version of quarterly planning. Aligns with EOS Rocks but adds a scaling lens.

**Step 1: Score the past 90 days.** What did you commit to? What actually happened? What worked and what didn't? Be honest.

**Step 2: Assess the Three-Legged Stool.** Rate Systems, Team, and Controls. Which leg is weakest right now?

**Step 3: Assess the Five Pillars.** Rate each pillar. Which is the bottleneck?

**Step 4: Run the "Hit by a Bus" Test.** What are the top 3 vulnerabilities?

**Step 5: Set 90-day priorities.** Based on the above, choose 3-5 priorities for the next quarter. At least one should address the weakest leg and at least one should address the weakest pillar.

**Step 6: Create action plans.** For each priority, define: What specifically will be done? Who owns it? What does "done" look like? What resources are needed?

**Step 7: Set the review rhythm.** Weekly check-in on progress. Monthly deeper review. Quarterly full cycle.

### Time Mastery Strategies

For the owner of a small firm, how you spend your time IS the strategy. These are the rules:

1. **Track your time for two weeks.** Categorize every hour as: (a) $10/hour work (admin, scheduling, filing), (b) $100/hour work (client delivery, project management), (c) $1,000/hour work (sales, strategy, key relationships, team development), (d) $10,000/hour work (vision, partnerships, big deals, business model innovation).

2. **Shift up the value ladder.** The goal is to spend 80%+ of your time on $1,000 and $10,000 work. Delegate or eliminate the rest.

3. **Protect strategic time.** Block 2-4 hours weekly for uninterrupted business-building work. This is non-negotiable. It goes on the calendar before anything else.

4. **Batch similar work.** Don't interleave client delivery with sales calls with admin. Batch work by type and do it in dedicated blocks.

5. **Create an "owner's ideal week" template.** Map out what a perfect week looks like in terms of time allocation. Compare actual weeks to the ideal. Close the gap.

**CTRS Application:** The owner of a market intelligence firm is typically very good at research and analysis ($100/hour work) and gets pulled into it because it's comfortable. The highest-leverage shift is from doing the analysis to building the team, systems, and relationships that generate analysis at scale ($1,000-$10,000/hour work).

## 4. Kotter's 8-Step Change Process

Source: John P. Kotter, *Leading Change*.

Use this when CTRS needs to make a significant shift -- entering a new market, restructuring the team, adopting a new methodology, changing the service model, or any change that requires people to behave differently. In a small firm, change is easier to execute but harder to sustain because there's less infrastructure to reinforce new behaviors.

### Phase 1: Creating a Climate for Change

#### Step 1: Create a sense of urgency

**What to do:** Help the team see why the change is necessary NOW. Not next quarter, not eventually -- now. Complacency is the enemy.

**How to do it at CTRS:**
- Share specific data: declining win rates, client feedback, competitive moves, market shifts.
- Quantify the cost of not changing: "If we stay on this trajectory, here's what happens in 12 months."
- Bring outside voices in: Share client quotes, competitor announcements, industry reports.
- Remove false comfort: If "things are fine" is the default answer, challenge it with facts.

**Danger:** In a small firm, urgency can tip into panic. The goal is motivated urgency, not fear. Frame it as opportunity, not crisis, when possible.

#### Step 2: Build a guiding coalition

**What to do:** Assemble a group with enough power, credibility, and energy to lead the change.

**How to do it at CTRS:**
- In a 2-5 person firm, the "coalition" may be just 2-3 people. That's fine -- but you need at least one person besides the owner who champions the change.
- Identify who on the team is most likely to embrace this change. Get them involved in designing it, not just executing it.
- If everyone resists, the change may be wrong, or the case for urgency hasn't been made.

#### Step 3: Form a strategic vision and initiatives

**What to do:** Create a clear picture of what the future looks like after the change and what the first concrete steps are.

**How to do it at CTRS:**
- Write a 1-paragraph vision of the changed state: "After this change, CTRS will ___."
- Define 3-5 specific initiatives that make the change real.
- Ensure the vision connects to the team's self-interest: How does this make their work better, not just harder?

### Phase 2: Engaging and Enabling the Whole Organization

#### Step 4: Enlist a volunteer army

**What to do:** Communicate the vision relentlessly and get broad buy-in.

**How to do it at CTRS:**
- In a small firm, "enlist" means having genuine conversations, not sending memos. Sit down with each team member individually.
- Ask for their input and concerns. Adjust the plan where input improves it.
- Repeat the "why" in every meeting. People need to hear the reason for change 7+ times before it sticks.

#### Step 5: Enable action by removing barriers

**What to do:** Remove obstacles that prevent people from acting on the vision. These can be structural, systemic, or human.

**How to do it at CTRS:**
- **Structural barriers:** Does the Accountability Chart support the new way of working? Adjust roles if needed.
- **Skill barriers:** Does the team need training to execute the change? Provide it before expecting results.
- **System barriers:** Do current tools, processes, or incentives work against the change? Fix them first.
- **Human barriers:** Is someone actively undermining the change? In a small firm, one resister can poison the well. Address it directly and quickly.

#### Step 6: Generate short-term wins

**What to do:** Produce visible, unambiguous results quickly. This sustains momentum and silences skeptics.

**How to do it at CTRS:**
- Plan for wins within the first 30 days of the change.
- Make the wins visible: share them in the Level 10 Meeting, celebrate them.
- Connect the wins back to the change: "This happened because we made the shift."
- Examples: First client won with the new approach. First engagement delivered under the new process. First week where the Scorecard shows improvement.

### Phase 3: Implementing and Sustaining the Change

#### Step 7: Sustain acceleration

**What to do:** Don't let up after the first wins. Use the credibility from early wins to tackle deeper changes.

**How to do it at CTRS:**
- After each win, ask: "What's the next barrier to knock down?"
- Continue hiring, promoting, and developing people who embody the change.
- Resist the temptation to declare victory too early. The change isn't done until it's how things are, not how things are being tried.

#### Step 8: Institute the change in culture

**What to do:** Anchor the change in the way the organization operates. Make the new way the default.

**How to do it at CTRS:**
- Update the documented processes (Three-Step Process Documenter) to reflect the new way.
- Update the Scorecard to measure what matters under the new model.
- Hire for the new model's requirements.
- Tell the story of the change: "We used to ___, and we changed because ___. Now we ___." This narrative becomes part of the culture.
- For CTRS specifically: Update the V/TO to reflect any changes in Core Focus, Three Uniques, or Marketing Strategy.

## 5. Blue Ocean Tipping Point Leadership

Source: Kim & Mauborgne, *Blue Ocean Strategy* and *Blue Ocean Shift*.

Tipping Point Leadership is about executing strategic change rapidly in an organization, even when resources are limited. For CTRS, it addresses the question: "How do we make a big strategic shift with a small team and limited budget?"

### The Four Organizational Hurdles

Any major strategic shift faces four hurdles. Traditional approaches throw resources at all four simultaneously. Tipping Point Leadership identifies the tipping point in each hurdle and focuses effort there.

#### Hurdle 1: Cognitive

**The problem:** People don't see the need for change. They're comfortable with the status quo.

**Solution: Make people SEE the need, don't tell them.**
- **Ride the electric sewer.** Put people face-to-face with the problem. Don't share reports about client dissatisfaction -- have team members sit in on a difficult client call. Don't describe competitive threats -- show the competitor's actual deliverable side-by-side with yours.
- **Meet with disgruntled customers.** If clients are leaving, find out why in person. Have team members hear it directly.
- **For CTRS:** Bring the team into the pipeline review. Show them the proposals you lost and why. Let them see the competitive landscape firsthand. Share real client feedback verbatim. Experience is more persuasive than data.

#### Hurdle 2: Resource

**The problem:** You don't have the budget, time, or people to execute the change.

**Solution: Multiply the value of existing resources.**
- **Hot spots:** Identify the activities that consume few resources but produce high results. Shift resources toward these.
- **Cold spots:** Identify the activities that consume high resources but produce low results. Eliminate or reduce these.
- **Horse trading:** Exchange resources you have too much of for resources you need.
- **For CTRS:** Audit where time is spent. In most small consulting firms, 20% of activities generate 80% of value. Common cold spots: over-servicing unprofitable clients, internal meetings without agendas, research that exceeds what the client needs. Common hot spots: the specific deliverable format clients rave about, the onboarding process that sets up engagement success, the follow-up that generates repeat business.

#### Hurdle 3: Motivational

**The problem:** People see the need and you have the resources, but they won't move.

**Solution: Focus on kingpins, fishbowl management, and atomization.**
- **Kingpins:** In every team, there are 1-2 people who are natural influencers. Win them over first and the rest follow. In a small firm, identify who the team listens to (it may not be the most senior person).
- **Fishbowl management:** Make performance transparent. Put the Scorecard on a shared dashboard. When everyone can see everyone's numbers, social pressure drives performance.
- **Atomization:** Break the change into small, concrete steps that feel achievable. "Transform our delivery model" is paralyzing. "This week, we pilot the new intake template with one client" is doable.
- **For CTRS:** The owner is likely the biggest kingpin. If the owner visibly changes their own behavior (delegating more, following the new process, using the Scorecard), the team follows. Model the change before mandating it.

#### Hurdle 4: Political

**The problem:** Powerful opponents actively resist the change.

**Solution: Secure a consigliere and silence the opposition early.**
- **Identify the opponents:** Who loses power, comfort, or resources if the change succeeds? In a small firm, this might be someone whose role is threatened or who was hired to do things the old way.
- **Secure a consigliere:** Find someone respected by the team who supports the change and can help you navigate resistance. In a small firm, this might be an advisor, board member, or key client.
- **Isolate the opponents:** Don't fight them publicly. Address concerns privately. If someone is actively sabotaging the change, deal with it as a People issue (People Analyzer).
- **Build a coalition of supporters first.** By the time opponents mobilize, the change should already have momentum.
- **For CTRS:** Political hurdles in a small firm are more personal than organizational. A team member who resists change may need a direct, compassionate conversation about whether this is still the right company for them. Don't let one person's resistance hold back the entire firm.

### Fair Process

Fair Process is the principle that makes change execution sustainable. People are more likely to support even difficult changes if the process is fair.

**Three elements:**

**1. Engagement:** Involve people in the decisions that affect them. Ask for their input. You don't have to take every suggestion, but you must genuinely listen and consider it.

**How to apply at CTRS:**
- Include team members in the quarterly planning session, at least for the portions that affect their work.
- When setting Rocks, ask each person what they think the priorities should be before the owner dictates them.
- When redesigning a process, involve the people who execute it.

**2. Explanation:** When a decision is made, explain the reasoning behind it. People can accept outcomes they disagree with if they understand why.

**How to apply at CTRS:**
- "We're focusing on this vertical because [specific reasons] and not that one because [specific reasons]."
- "We're changing the delivery process because [what's not working] and the new approach addresses it by [how]."
- Never use "because I said so" as a reason. In a small firm, authority-based decisions destroy trust.

**3. Expectation Clarity:** Make sure everyone knows what is expected of them under the new rules. What are the new standards? How will performance be measured? What happens if expectations aren't met?

**How to apply at CTRS:**
- After any change, update role expectations in writing.
- Revise the Scorecard metrics if needed.
- Set specific, measurable Rocks for the transition.
- Be explicit about what "good" looks like under the new model.

## 6. RAPID Decision Model

Source: Bain & Company (Paul Rogers and Marcia Blenko).

RAPID clarifies who does what in a decision. In a small firm, many decisions are murky -- the owner makes them all, or nobody knows who has authority. RAPID assigns five roles for each major decision.

### The Five Roles

**R -- Recommend**
- The person (or people) who gather data, analyze options, and propose a course of action.
- They frame the decision and present alternatives with a clear recommendation.
- They do NOT make the final call.
- For CTRS: Typically a team member with relevant expertise or the person closest to the situation.

**A -- Agree**
- The person (or people) who must formally agree with the recommendation before it moves forward.
- This is a veto -- if A says no, the recommendation goes back to R for revision.
- Use this role sparingly. Multiple A's create gridlock.
- For CTRS: Use for decisions with significant financial, client, or strategic risk. The person with the most at stake should be in the A role.

**P -- Perform**
- The person (or people) who execute the decision once it's made.
- They are responsible for implementation, not for the decision itself.
- For CTRS: The team member(s) assigned to carry out the action.

**I -- Input**
- The person (or people) whose input is sought before the decision is made.
- Their input is valued but not binding. The decision can proceed without their agreement.
- For CTRS: Team members with relevant expertise, key clients (for client-facing decisions), advisors.

**D -- Decide**
- The single person who makes the final decision.
- There is exactly one D for every decision. Never two. Never a committee.
- The D has the authority to override the recommendation but should do so rarely and with explanation.
- For CTRS: The owner for strategic decisions; the relevant seat-holder for operational decisions within their domain.

### Rules for Assignment

1. **One D per decision.** If there are two decision-makers, there are zero decision-makers.
2. **The D should be the person closest to the action who has the appropriate authority level.** Push decision rights down as far as possible.
3. **Minimize the number of A's.** Every A is a veto that slows the process. Use only when the risk justifies it.
4. **I's must provide input promptly.** If input is requested and not provided within the deadline, the decision proceeds without it.
5. **R's own the quality of the analysis.** A bad recommendation is the R's responsibility, not the D's.
6. **P's should be involved early.** Don't decide first and then throw it over the wall to the people who have to implement it.

### CTRS Decision Categories

Map your recurring decisions to RAPID roles:

| Decision Type | R | A | P | I | D |
|--------------|---|---|---|---|---|
| New client acceptance | Sales lead | Owner (for fit) | Engagement team | Finance (capacity) | Owner |
| Engagement pricing | Sales lead | Owner | N/A | Finance | Owner |
| Hiring | Hiring manager | Owner | HR/Admin | Team members | Owner |
| Methodology change | Senior analyst | Owner | Delivery team | Affected clients | Owner |
| Marketing spend | Marketing lead | Owner | Marketing lead | Finance | Owner |
| Day-to-day delivery decisions | Analyst on engagement | N/A | Analyst | Engagement lead | Engagement lead |
| Client escalation | Engagement lead | N/A | Engagement lead | Owner | Owner |

**As CTRS grows:** Push D roles away from the owner and toward seat-holders. The owner should only be D for strategic, financial, and team decisions. Operational D's should belong to the team.

## 7. Strategy Execution Building Blocks

Source: Neilson, Martin, & Powers (HBR/Booz Allen research).

Research on thousands of organizations found that four building blocks determine whether a strategy gets executed. They are listed here in order of importance -- this order matters.

### Building Block 1: Decision Rights (Most Important)

**What it means:** Everyone knows who has the authority to make which decisions, and those decision rights are appropriate.

**Diagnostic questions for CTRS:**
- Do team members know which decisions they can make without asking the owner?
- Are decisions made at the right level, or does everything escalate to the top?
- Do decisions stick, or are they relitigated repeatedly?
- When two people disagree, is there a clear mechanism for resolution?

**How to strengthen it:**
- Map major decisions using RAPID (above).
- Explicitly communicate decision authority for each seat in the Accountability Chart.
- Create a simple decision rights matrix: which decisions require owner approval, which require notification only, and which can be made independently.
- Resist the urge to override decisions you've delegated. If you take back a decision after delegating it, you've destroyed the delegation.

### Building Block 2: Information (Second Most Important)

**What it means:** Information flows freely to the people who need it when they need it. People on the front lines can see the data they need to make good decisions. Leadership can see what's happening on the ground.

**Diagnostic questions for CTRS:**
- Does the team have access to client feedback, pipeline data, and financial metrics?
- Do client-facing team members know the strategic priorities well enough to make aligned decisions in real time?
- Does the owner know what's happening in active engagements without having to ask for updates?
- Is information shared proactively or only when requested?

**How to strengthen it:**
- The Scorecard (weekly numbers visible to all) is the primary tool.
- Ensure the Level 10 Meeting includes the Customer/Employee Headlines segment -- this is a structured information-sharing mechanism.
- Use a shared project management system where engagement status is visible to everyone.
- Default to transparency. In a small firm, there's rarely a reason to withhold information from the team.

### Building Block 3: Motivators (Third Most Important)

**What it means:** Incentives, rewards, and consequences align with strategic objectives. People are motivated to execute the strategy, not just to show up.

**Diagnostic questions for CTRS:**
- Does compensation reward the behaviors that drive the strategy? Or does it reward hours regardless of outcomes?
- Are high performers recognized and rewarded?
- Are low performers addressed, or are they tolerated to avoid conflict?
- Do team members see a path for personal growth that aligns with the company's direction?

**How to strengthen it:**
- Tie a portion of compensation to Rock completion and Scorecard targets.
- Recognize people publicly in Level 10 Meetings when they exemplify core values or achieve significant results.
- Address People Analyzer issues promptly. Tolerating someone who doesn't meet the bar demoralizes everyone who does.
- For a small firm: intrinsic motivation matters more than complex incentive systems. People want meaningful work, autonomy in their domain, the opportunity to grow, and to work with people they respect. Ensure CTRS provides all four.

### Building Block 4: Structure (Least Important -- but Still Necessary)

**What it means:** The organizational structure supports the strategy -- reporting lines, role definitions, and team configuration.

**Diagnostic questions for CTRS:**
- Does the Accountability Chart reflect the strategy, or does it reflect history?
- Are roles defined by what the business needs, or by what the people currently do?
- Is there too much hierarchy (slows decisions) or too little (creates chaos)?
- Does the structure support the key capabilities identified in the strategic cascade?

**How to strengthen it:**
- Rebuild the Accountability Chart from the strategy down, not from the current team up.
- In a small firm, keep structure flat. Two layers maximum: owner/leadership and everyone else.
- Revisit structure when the strategy changes. Don't let structure become calcified.

**Critical insight:** Most organizations try to fix execution problems by restructuring. This research shows that restructuring is the LEAST effective lever. Fix decision rights and information flow first. Only restructure when the other three building blocks are in place and structure is genuinely the remaining constraint.

## 8. McKinsey 7S Model

Source: Peters & Waterman / McKinsey & Company.

The 7S Model assesses organizational alignment. When execution fails, it's often because the seven elements are working against each other. Use this as a diagnostic: find the misalignment, fix it, and execution improves.

### The Seven Elements

**Hard elements** (easier to define and manage):

**1. Strategy**
- The plan for achieving competitive advantage.
- For CTRS: The Lafley/Martin cascade choices documented in the V/TO. Where-to-play and how-to-win.
- **Assessment question:** Is our strategy clearly articulated and understood by every team member?

**2. Structure**
- The organizational hierarchy and reporting relationships.
- For CTRS: The Accountability Chart with defined seats, roles, and reporting lines.
- **Assessment question:** Does our structure support our strategy, or does it create friction?

**3. Systems**
- The daily processes, workflows, and routines that define how work gets done.
- For CTRS: Core processes (delivery, sales, quality review), the Level 10 Meeting, the Scorecard, the Rocks process.
- **Assessment question:** Do our systems reinforce the strategy, or do they incentivize old behaviors?

**Soft elements** (harder to define, often more powerful):

**4. Shared Values** (center of the model -- connects to all others)
- The core beliefs and attitudes that shape the organization's culture.
- For CTRS: The core values discovered through the V/TO process.
- **Assessment question:** Are our stated values actually lived? Would an outsider observe these values in action?

**5. Skills**
- The distinctive competencies that reside in the organization.
- For CTRS: Research methodology expertise, analytical capabilities, client management, industry knowledge.
- **Assessment question:** Do we have the skills required by our strategy? Where are the gaps?

**6. Style**
- The leadership and management style of the organization.
- For CTRS: How the owner leads, how decisions are made, how conflict is handled, how mistakes are treated.
- **Assessment question:** Does our leadership style attract and retain the people we need? Does it support or undermine the strategy?

**7. Staff**
- The people, their backgrounds, competencies, and how they are developed.
- For CTRS: The team assessed through the People Analyzer and GWC.
- **Assessment question:** Do we have the right people? Are we developing them in line with the strategy?

### How to Assess Alignment

**Step 1:** Rate each element independently on a 1-10 scale for strength.

**Step 2:** For each pair of elements, ask: "Does element A support or conflict with element B?" Look for misalignments.

**Common misalignment patterns for a small firm:**

| Misalignment | Symptom | Fix |
|-------------|---------|-----|
| Strategy vs. Structure | The strategy requires specialization but the structure has everyone doing everything | Redesign the Accountability Chart to match the strategy |
| Strategy vs. Skills | The strategy targets a new market but no one has expertise there | Invest in training or hire for the gap before committing |
| Strategy vs. Systems | The strategy emphasizes speed but the processes are built for thoroughness | Redesign processes to match the strategic priority |
| Shared Values vs. Style | The values emphasize transparency but the owner makes decisions unilaterally | Adopt Fair Process principles (see above) |
| Structure vs. Staff | The structure requires a manager but no one on the team has management experience | Develop existing team or hire for the gap |
| Systems vs. Staff | The systems assume high autonomy but the staff needs more guidance | Invest in training or simplify systems |

**Step 3:** Identify the 1-2 most damaging misalignments. Address these before trying to improve any individual element. Alignment matters more than the strength of any single element.

## 9. Greiner's Growth Model

Source: Larry Greiner, "Evolution and Revolution as Organizations Grow."

Organizations grow through predictable phases, each ending in a crisis that must be resolved to reach the next phase. Use this model to identify which phase CTRS is in, anticipate the coming crisis, and prepare for it before it hits.

### Phase 1: Growth Through Creativity

**Characteristics:**
- The founder does everything. Product/service development, sales, delivery, admin.
- Communication is informal and frequent.
- Long hours, modest pay, promise of future rewards.
- The business succeeds because of the founder's energy and talent.

**Crisis: Leadership**
- The business outgrows what one person can manage.
- The founder is overwhelmed by management responsibilities they didn't sign up for and may not be good at.
- The business needs professional management, but the founder resists giving up control.

**Resolution:** Hire or develop a management capability. The founder must shift from doing to leading. In EOS terms, the Visionary must find or become an Integrator.

**CTRS indicator you're in Phase 1:** The owner is the primary doer of client work AND the primary seller AND the primary manager. There's no one else who can run the business for a week.

### Phase 2: Growth Through Direction

**Characteristics:**
- A more formal organizational structure emerges.
- Roles become specialized (some people sell, some deliver, some manage).
- Communication becomes more formal -- there are systems and meetings.
- The business runs more efficiently but the hierarchy grows.

**Crisis: Autonomy**
- Team members who were hired to specialize become frustrated by centralized decision-making.
- The leadership team (or owner) is the bottleneck for every decision.
- Good people leave because they feel micromanaged or constrained.

**Resolution:** Delegate real authority. Push decision rights down (RAPID model). Accept that others will make decisions differently than you would. In EOS terms, strengthen the Accountability Chart so each seat has genuine authority over its domain.

**CTRS indicator you're in Phase 2:** The owner has a team but approves everything. Team members wait for the owner's input before moving forward. Decisions pile up on the owner's desk.

### Phase 3: Growth Through Delegation

**Characteristics:**
- Middle managers (or team leads) have real authority.
- The owner focuses on strategy and exceptions.
- Motivation improves because people have ownership.
- The business can grow without the owner being in every conversation.

**Crisis: Control**
- Decentralized decisions lead to inconsistency.
- The owner feels out of touch with what's happening.
- Quality varies because different people make different choices.
- Financial or client problems surface that the owner didn't see coming.

**Resolution:** Implement coordination and control systems WITHOUT reverting to centralized authority. The Scorecard, Level 10 Meeting, and documented processes (EOS tools) are exactly the right mechanisms. Control through systems, not through personal oversight.

**CTRS indicator you're in Phase 3:** The owner has delegated but worries about quality. Client deliverables sometimes don't meet the owner's standards. Financial surprises happen. The team is capable but inconsistent.

### Phase 4: Growth Through Coordination

**Characteristics:**
- Formal systems for planning, reporting, and quality control.
- The business runs predictably.
- Data-driven decision-making.
- Cross-functional coordination works smoothly.

**Crisis: Red Tape**
- Systems and processes that once helped now slow things down.
- Innovation is stifled by procedure.
- Bureaucracy grows faster than the business.
- Good people feel trapped by process.

**Resolution:** Simplify. Return to the EOS principle of simplicity. Eliminate any process that doesn't directly serve clients or strategy. Empower teams to work around bureaucracy when speed matters. For CTRS, this crisis is far in the future but worth noting: never build processes for their own sake.

**CTRS indicator you're in Phase 4:** Unlikely at current size. This typically occurs at 50+ employees.

### Phase 5: Growth Through Collaboration

**Characteristics:**
- Team-based structure. Flexible, adaptable.
- Focus on problem-solving and innovation.
- Reduced formal hierarchy.
- Matrix-like structures, cross-functional teams.

**Crisis: Internal Growth**
- The organization has exploited its internal capabilities and needs external partnerships, mergers, or new models to grow further.

**Resolution:** Strategic alliances, acquisitions, or business model reinvention.

**CTRS indicator you're in Phase 5:** Not applicable at current size.

### Using the Model

**Step 1:** Identify which phase CTRS is in currently. Most 2-10 person firms are in Phase 1 (late) or Phase 2 (early).

**Step 2:** Identify which crisis is approaching or already here.

**Step 3:** Implement the resolution BEFORE the crisis becomes acute. The firms that grow smoothly are the ones that prepare for the next phase during the current one.

**Step 4:** Don't skip phases. Each phase builds capabilities needed for the next. Trying to jump from Phase 1 to Phase 3 without building the management capability of Phase 2 will fail.

## 10. Porter's Tests of Consistency

Source: Michael Porter, *Competitive Strategy*.

Before committing to any strategic plan, run it through these four tests. If the strategy fails any test, revise it before investing in execution. These tests are the final quality gate at the end of a planning session.

### Test 1: Internal Consistency

**Question:** Are the goals, policies, and actions mutually consistent?

**What to check:**
- Do the Rocks reinforce each other, or do they compete for the same resources?
- Does the stated strategy match the actual resource allocation?
- Are the V/TO choices (where-to-play, how-to-win) consistent with the Three-Year Picture and One-Year Plan?
- Does the Accountability Chart support the strategy, or does it support the old strategy?

**Red flags:**
- Setting a Rock to "grow revenue 30%" while also setting a Rock to "reduce owner's client-facing hours by 50%." These may conflict unless you've built the capacity to replace the owner's delivery.
- Claiming differentiation through premium quality while setting pricing to undercut competitors.
- Targeting enterprise clients while structuring the team for high-volume, small engagements.

**How to fix:** Lay all strategic elements side by side. For each pair, ask: "Does A support B?" If the answer is no or unclear, something must change.

### Test 2: Environmental Fit

**Question:** Does the strategy exploit opportunities and address threats in the external environment?

**What to check:**
- Does the where-to-play choice target a segment that is growing, stable, or declining?
- Does the how-to-win theory match what buyers actually value?
- Has the competitive landscape shifted since the strategy was set?
- Are there technological, regulatory, or market changes that invalidate assumptions?

**Red flags:**
- Targeting a segment where the trend is toward in-house capabilities (the buyers are building what you sell).
- Relying on a competitive advantage that is being commoditized (e.g., data access that's becoming freely available).
- Ignoring a disruptive entrant that's winning budget at the lower end.

**How to fix:** Revisit the strategic diagnosis. If the environment has changed, the strategy must change. Use the Five Forces check and PESTEL scan from the analysis-frameworks reference.

### Test 3: Resource Fit

**Question:** Does the strategy match the resources and capabilities available?

**What to check:**
- Do you have the people (skills and capacity) to execute the strategy?
- Do you have the financial resources to fund the strategy through execution?
- Do you have the time? (In a small firm, the owner's time is the most constrained resource.)
- Do you have the technology, tools, and infrastructure?

**Red flags:**
- A strategy that requires 5 full-time equivalents when you have 3.
- A plan that depends on hiring someone you haven't found yet, on a timeline that assumes they start tomorrow.
- Rocks that collectively require 120% of the team's capacity.
- A strategy that requires cash investment you don't have and haven't secured.

**How to fix:** Either scale back the strategy to match current resources, or acquire the resources first and then pursue the strategy. Never assume resources will appear. Resource-constrained firms (like CTRS) succeed by doing fewer things at higher quality, not by stretching thin across too many initiatives.

### Test 4: Communication and Implementation

**Question:** Can the strategy be communicated clearly enough to guide day-to-day decisions, and can it actually be implemented?

**What to check:**
- Can every team member articulate the strategy in one sentence?
- If a team member faces a decision not covered by explicit rules, does the strategy give them enough guidance to choose correctly?
- Are the Rocks specific enough to be executed? (Vague Rocks indicate a strategy that hasn't been translated into action.)
- Is the meeting rhythm (Level 10, quarterly sessions) sufficient to keep the strategy alive?

**Red flags:**
- Team members can recite the V/TO but make day-to-day decisions that contradict it.
- The strategy uses language that sounds good in a planning session but doesn't guide action ("be the best," "delight clients," "leverage our capabilities").
- The implementation plan has no milestones between now and the 90-day mark.
- No one besides the owner can explain why CTRS is pursuing this strategy.

**How to fix:** Simplify the strategy language until it's actionable. Test comprehension by asking each team member to describe the strategy and then describe how their next week's work connects to it. If there's a gap, close it through conversation and clarity, not more documentation.

### Running the Four Tests: Step-by-Step

1. Write the strategy on one page: where-to-play, how-to-win, capabilities, and this quarter's Rocks.
2. Run Test 1: Check each element against every other element. Mark conflicts.
3. Run Test 2: Compare the strategy to the latest environmental diagnosis. Mark assumptions that have changed.
4. Run Test 3: List every resource the strategy requires. Mark gaps.
5. Run Test 4: Hand the strategy to a team member who wasn't in the planning session. Ask them to explain it back and describe what they'd do differently next week based on it. Mark confusion points.
6. Revise the strategy to resolve every mark before committing to execution.
