## 1. Lafley/Martin Strategic Choice Cascade

Source: *Playing to Win* (A.G. Lafley and Roger Martin)

Strategy is an integrated set of choices that positions the firm to win. Not a vision statement, not a plan, not a set of goals -- a set of five interconnected choices.

### The Five Questions

Work through these in order. Each choice constrains and enables the next.

**Question 1: What is our winning aspiration?**

Define what winning means for CTRS and its clients. This is not a financial target. It is the purpose and motivating aspiration for the enterprise.

- Ask: "What does it look like when we are winning?"
- Ask: "What impact are we having on clients when we are at our best?"
- Ask: "What would we need to be true to say we are the best at what we do?"
- Frame it in terms of client outcomes, not internal metrics.
- Example for a MI firm: "We are the firm that gives mid-market companies the intelligence advantage that was previously only available to the Fortune 500."

**Question 2: Where will we play?**

Define the specific playing field. Every dimension you choose is a dimension where you will NOT play elsewhere. Force trade-offs.

Dimensions to define:
- **Client segments**: Which industries? Which company sizes? Which buyer personas (CEO, CMO, VP Strategy, Board)?
- **Geographies**: Local, regional, national, international? Which specific markets?
- **Service types**: What specific deliverables? Competitive analysis, market sizing, customer research, due diligence, trend forecasting, strategic advisory?
- **Engagement types**: Project-based, retainer, embedded, advisory board?
- **Channels**: How do clients find and engage you? Direct sales, referral, content marketing, partnerships?
- **Price tier**: Premium, mid-market, accessible?

Key questions to force specificity:
- "If a prospect outside these boundaries calls, what do we say?"
- "Which clients would we fire to make room for better-fit clients?"
- "Where are we currently playing that we should stop?"

**Question 3: How will we win where we have chosen to play?**

Define the theory of competitive advantage. There are only two fundamental ways to win:

1. **Cost leadership** -- Deliver comparable value at lower cost (not lower price; lower cost to serve). For a services firm, this means: more efficient methodologies, better tooling, leaner delivery, templatized offerings.

2. **Differentiation** -- Deliver unique value that justifies a price premium. For a services firm, this means: deeper expertise, proprietary methods, superior client experience, better talent, unique data access, specialized domain knowledge.

Pick one primary lever. Support it with the other where possible, but do not try to win on both simultaneously.

Questions to clarify how-to-win:
- "Why do clients choose us over alternatives?"
- "What can we do that competitors cannot easily replicate?"
- "If we raised prices 20%, would clients stay? Why or why not?"
- "What is the 'secret sauce' that makes our work better?"

**Question 4: What capabilities must be in place?**

Identify the 3-5 reinforcing capabilities that deliver the where-to-play and how-to-win choices. These are activities the firm must perform consistently and at a high level.

For a MI firm, candidates include:
- Deep domain expertise in chosen industries
- Proprietary research methodology or analytical frameworks
- Client relationship management and advisory skill
- Speed and responsiveness of delivery
- Technology/data infrastructure
- Talent recruitment and development
- Thought leadership and market presence

Ask: "Which capabilities are table stakes (everyone needs them) vs. differentiating (they make our strategy work)?"

Build a reinforcing activity system: capabilities should strengthen each other. If you invest in proprietary methodology AND deep domain expertise, each makes the other more valuable.

**Question 5: What management systems are required?**

Define the systems that ensure the strategy is implemented and reinforced daily.

- **Measurement systems**: What metrics track whether the strategy is working? (Client retention in target segments, win rate on target engagements, utilization on strategic work vs. off-strategy work)
- **Decision rules**: How do we evaluate new opportunities? (Does it fit where-to-play? Does it leverage how-to-win?)
- **Capability-building systems**: How do we invest in the critical capabilities? (Training, hiring criteria, tool investment)
- **Communication systems**: How does the team know and reinforce the strategy? (Meeting rhythms, onboarding, strategy artifacts)

### The Two Ways to Win (Expanded)

**Cost Leadership for a Services Firm:**
- Develop templatized deliverables and reusable frameworks
- Build internal knowledge bases that reduce research time
- Invest in tools and automation that lower per-project cost
- Standardize engagement scoping and delivery processes
- Hire for efficiency and train on methodology
- Risk: commoditization pressure, talent retention issues, race to the bottom

**Differentiation for a Services Firm:**
- Develop proprietary intellectual property (frameworks, databases, methods)
- Hire and retain deep domain experts
- Invest in client relationships and advisory positioning
- Produce thought leadership that demonstrates unique insight
- Customize solutions rather than deliver off-the-shelf
- Charge premium pricing and justify it with superior outcomes
- Risk: higher costs, scalability limits, key-person dependencies

### The Reverse Engineering Process

Use this when evaluating a specific strategic option -- a new service line, a new market, a partnership, an acquisition, a major investment. It replaces debates about whether something is a good idea with a structured analysis of what must be true.

**Step 1: Frame the choice.**

State the decision as two or more mutually exclusive options. Not "Should we do X?" but "Should we do X or Y?" Always include the status quo as an option.

Example: "Should we (a) launch a subscription intelligence product for mid-market PE firms, (b) double down on custom MI engagements for our current client base, or (c) continue as-is?"

**Step 2: Generate strategic possibilities.**

For each option, articulate the full story of how it would work. What would the offering look like? Who would buy it? How would it be delivered? What would the economics be? How would it fit with existing operations?

Do not evaluate yet. Just flesh out each possibility into a coherent narrative.

**Step 3: Specify conditions -- "What would have to be true?"**

For each possibility, ask: "What would have to be true about the following for this option to be a winning choice?"

Work through these categories:
- **Industry/market conditions**: What would have to be true about the market size, growth, competitive dynamics?
- **Channel conditions**: What would have to be true about how we reach and sell to these clients?
- **Customer conditions**: What would have to be true about what clients need, value, and will pay for?
- **Capability conditions**: What would have to be true about our ability to deliver?
- **Cost/economic conditions**: What would have to be true about the unit economics, investment required, and payoff timeline?

List every condition. Be exhaustive.

**Step 4: Identify barriers to choice.**

Review the conditions for each possibility. Which conditions are you LEAST confident about? Which ones, if they turned out to be false, would kill the option?

Rank conditions by: (a) how uncertain you are, and (b) how consequential they are. The conditions that are both highly uncertain and highly consequential are the barriers.

**Step 5: Design tests.**

For each barrier condition, design a test that would increase your confidence. Tests should be:
- **Fast**: Weeks, not months
- **Cheap**: Minimum viable investment
- **Decisive**: The result would meaningfully change your confidence

Types of tests:
- Customer interviews (5-10 target buyers)
- Small-scale pilots or MVPs
- Competitive analysis (who else is doing this, and how is it going?)
- Financial modeling with sensitivity analysis
- Expert interviews (people who have done something similar)
- Market data analysis

**Step 6: Conduct tests.**

Run the tests in order of the most critical barrier conditions first. If the most important condition fails, you do not need to test the rest.

**Step 7: Make the choice.**

With test results in hand, revisit the conditions. Which option has the fewest remaining barriers? Which has the strongest evidence that its conditions hold?

Make the choice. Commit to it. Set the Rocks to execute it.

## 2. Porter's Three Generic Strategies

Source: *Competitive Strategy* and *Competitive Advantage* (Michael Porter)

There are three viable strategic positions. Firms that fail to commit to one become "stuck in the middle" and underperform.

### Cost Leadership

Be the lowest-cost producer in the industry (not necessarily the lowest price -- the lowest cost to serve).

**How to apply in a services firm:**
- Standardize deliverables into repeatable, efficient products
- Build internal knowledge bases and reusable research assets
- Invest in technology that reduces labor per engagement
- Hire junior analysts and train them on efficient methodology
- Target high-volume, lower-complexity engagements
- Price competitively and make it up on volume and efficiency

**When this fits CTRS:** When the target market values speed and affordability over depth, when engagements can be standardized, when technology creates genuine cost advantages.

### Differentiation

Offer something uniquely valuable that clients will pay a premium for.

**How to apply in a services firm:**
- Develop proprietary frameworks, databases, or analytical approaches
- Hire recognized experts with domain credibility
- Invest in deep client relationships and advisory positioning
- Deliver customized, high-touch engagements
- Produce thought leadership that establishes authority
- Price at a premium and defend it with superior outcomes

**When this fits CTRS:** When serving clients who face high-stakes decisions and value insight quality over cost, when the firm has genuine expertise advantages, when relationships and trust are central to the sale.

### Focus (Cost Focus or Differentiation Focus)

Apply either cost leadership or differentiation to a narrow market segment rather than the broad market.

**How to apply in a services firm:**
- Choose a specific industry vertical (e.g., healthcare, fintech, defense)
- Choose a specific type of intelligence (e.g., only competitive intelligence, only market entry analysis)
- Choose a specific buyer (e.g., only private equity firms doing due diligence)
- Become the undisputed expert in that narrow domain
- Build all infrastructure, knowledge, and relationships around that focus

**When this fits CTRS:** Almost always. Small firms rarely have the resources to compete broadly. Focus is the natural strategy for a 2-10 person firm. The question is not whether to focus but where to focus.

### The Danger of Being Stuck in the Middle

A firm is stuck in the middle when it:
- Tries to serve all client types with all service types
- Has no clear basis of advantage (neither cheapest nor most differentiated)
- Wins deals on relationships or luck rather than a repeatable strategic logic
- Cannot articulate in one sentence why a client should choose them over alternatives

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "Can we describe our competitive advantage in one sentence?"
- "Are we winning clients because of our strategy or despite the lack of one?"
- "If our top salesperson left, would clients still choose us?"
- "Are we competing on price in some deals and quality in others, without a clear pattern?"

If CTRS is stuck in the middle, the quarterly planning session must address this before setting Rocks.

## 3. Blue Ocean Strategy Tools

Source: *Blue Ocean Strategy* (W. Chan Kim and Renee Mauborgne)

Blue Ocean Strategy is about creating uncontested market space rather than competing head-to-head in crowded markets. For a MI firm, this means finding ways to deliver intelligence value that existing competitors do not offer, rather than fighting over the same RFPs.

### Value Innovation

The cornerstone concept. Value innovation occurs when a firm simultaneously pursues differentiation AND low cost by eliminating and reducing factors the industry competes on while raising and creating factors the industry has never offered.

This is NOT about being better at what competitors already do. It is about redefining what you offer so that the comparison becomes irrelevant.

**For CTRS, ask:**
- "What does the MI industry compete on that clients don't actually value much?"
- "What do clients struggle with that no MI firm addresses?"
- "How could we deliver intelligence in a way that is both more valuable AND less costly to produce?"

### The Strategy Canvas

A visual tool for comparing your value proposition against competitors across the factors of competition.

**How to draw a strategy canvas:**

1. **Identify the factors of competition.** List the 6-12 factors that MI firms compete on. Examples: depth of analysis, breadth of coverage, speed of delivery, price, brand prestige, customization, visual presentation, methodology rigor, access to primary sources, domain expertise, actionability of recommendations, ongoing support.

2. **Rate each competitor** (including CTRS) on each factor from low to high. Use a simple 1-5 scale.

3. **Plot the curves.** X-axis is the factors of competition. Y-axis is the offering level (low to high). Each competitor gets a line connecting their scores across all factors.

4. **Analyze the result:**
   - Where do all competitors look the same? (convergence = opportunity to diverge)
   - Where is CTRS below competitors? (fix or eliminate)
   - Where is CTRS above competitors? (potential differentiator -- or wasted investment)
   - What factors are missing from the canvas entirely? (creation opportunity)

5. **Draw the target curve.** This is the future state -- the value curve CTRS wants to have. It should look visibly different from competitors.

**A good strategy canvas has three qualities:**
- **Focus**: The curve has clear peaks and valleys, not a flat line
- **Divergence**: The curve has a different shape than competitors
- **Compelling tagline**: The strategy can be summarized in a phrase

### The Four Actions Framework (ERRC Grid)

Use this to redesign the value curve. For each factor of competition, ask one of four questions:

**Eliminate:** Which factors that the industry takes for granted should be eliminated entirely?
- "What do we include in deliverables that clients never read or use?"
- "What parts of our process exist because 'that's how MI is done' rather than because clients need them?"
- Example: Eliminate 100-page reports if clients only read the executive summary.

**Reduce:** Which factors should be reduced well below the industry standard?
- "Where are we over-delivering relative to what clients value?"
- "What are we spending time on that has diminishing returns?"
- Example: Reduce breadth of competitor coverage if clients only care about 3-5 key players.

**Raise:** Which factors should be raised well above the industry standard?
- "Where do clients consistently wish we did more?"
- "What factors, if dramatically improved, would change how clients perceive us?"
- Example: Raise actionability -- go beyond data to specific, prioritized recommendations.

**Create:** Which factors should be created that the industry has never offered?
- "What adjacent problems do clients face that no MI firm addresses?"
- "What would make our service feel indispensable rather than nice-to-have?"
- Example: Create an ongoing intelligence feed instead of point-in-time reports. Create strategic workshop facilitation bundled with research.

### ERRC Grid Format

| Eliminate | Raise |
|-----------|-------|
| Factor 1  | Factor 1 |
| Factor 2  | Factor 2 |

| Reduce    | Create |
|-----------|--------|
| Factor 1  | Factor 1 |
| Factor 2  | Factor 2 |

Fill this grid with specific factors. Each cell should have concrete items, not vague aspirations.

### The Six Paths to Reconstruct Market Boundaries

Use these paths to discover blue ocean opportunities. Each path challenges an assumption about how the industry defines its boundaries.

**Path 1: Look across alternative industries.**
- What alternatives (not just competitors) do clients use instead of MI services? Internal teams? Strategy consultants? Data platforms? News services? Personal networks?
- Ask: "What causes clients to choose an alternative over us? What would cause them to switch?"

**Path 2: Look across strategic groups within the industry.**
- What different tiers of MI firms exist? Boutique vs. Big Four advisory vs. platform/self-serve. What do clients gain and lose at each tier?
- Ask: "Can we combine the advantages of two tiers in a way nobody else does?"

**Path 3: Look across the chain of buyers.**
- Who actually uses the research (user)? Who pays for it (purchaser)? Who influences the decision (influencer)?
- Ask: "Are we selling to the purchaser but the user has different needs? Could we redesign for the actual user?"

**Path 4: Look across complementary product and service offerings.**
- What happens before, during, and after clients use MI deliverables?
- Ask: "What pain points exist in the total experience of using market intelligence? How could we address the whole journey?"

**Path 5: Look across functional or emotional appeal.**
- If MI firms compete on functional appeal (data, analysis, rigor), could CTRS add emotional appeal (confidence, peace of mind, strategic clarity)?
- Ask: "Are we selling data or are we selling better decisions?"

**Path 6: Look across time.**
- What trends are shaping the future of how companies use market intelligence? AI, real-time data, self-serve analytics, democratized research?
- Ask: "What will MI look like in 5 years? How can we start delivering that now?"

### The Strategic Sequence

Before committing to a blue ocean move, validate it through four sequential tests. Move to the next only if the current one passes.

**Test 1: Buyer Utility -- Is there exceptional buyer utility?**
- Does the offering deliver a leap in value for the buyer?
- Use the Buyer Utility Map: Cross-reference six utility levers (Customer Productivity, Simplicity, Convenience, Risk Reduction, Fun/Image, Environmental Friendliness) with six stages of the buyer experience cycle (Purchase, Delivery, Use, Supplements, Maintenance, Disposal).
- Identify where the new offering unlocks utility that was previously blocked.

**Test 2: Price -- Is the price accessible to the mass of target buyers?**
- Identify the price corridor of the mass: look at alternatives (different form, same function) and substitutes (different form, different function but same objective).
- Set a strategic price within this corridor. For a differentiated offering, price in the mid-to-upper range. For a market-creating offering, price for volume adoption.

**Test 3: Cost -- Can you produce at the strategic price and earn a profit?**
- Work backward from the strategic price to the required cost. Do not let costs determine price.
- Use the three levers: streamline operations, partner where you lack scale, change the pricing model (subscription, performance-based, etc.).

**Test 4: Adoption -- What are the adoption hurdles?**
- Identify resistance from three groups: employees (internal change), partners (business model impact), and the public/regulators (societal acceptance).
- Address each proactively before launch.

## 4. Osterwalder's Value Proposition Canvas

Source: *Value Proposition Design* (Alexander Osterwalder et al.)

Use this to ensure that CTRS's services are tightly aligned with what target clients actually need, want, and struggle with. This is the zoom-in tool -- it magnifies the fit between two blocks of the Business Model Canvas (Value Proposition and Customer Segment).

### Customer Profile (Right Side)

Build a detailed profile of the target client before designing the value proposition. For CTRS, build one profile per target client segment.

**Customer Jobs:** What is the client trying to get done?

Three types of jobs:
- **Functional jobs**: Specific tasks or problems. Examples: "Understand our competitive landscape," "Size a new market," "Evaluate an acquisition target," "Build a market entry strategy."
- **Social jobs**: How the client wants to be perceived. Examples: "Be seen as data-driven by the board," "Look prepared in investor meetings," "Demonstrate strategic rigor to partners."
- **Emotional jobs**: How the client wants to feel. Examples: "Feel confident in a major decision," "Reduce anxiety about an unfamiliar market," "Feel in control of competitive dynamics."

Also identify:
- **Supporting jobs**: Jobs in the context of purchasing and consuming your service. "Evaluate MI providers," "Onboard a new research partner," "Integrate findings into planning."

Trigger questions for customer jobs:
- "What is the one thing your target client cannot afford to get wrong?"
- "What tasks are they trying to complete when they search for MI services?"
- "What functional, social, or emotional outcomes are they seeking?"
- "What would they hire your service to do for them?" (Jobs-to-Be-Done framing)

**Customer Pains:** What makes the client frustrated, annoyed, or blocked before, during, or after getting the job done?

- **Undesired outcomes**: Getting analysis that is too late, too generic, too academic, or wrong
- **Obstacles**: Cannot find reliable data, internal team lacks expertise, time constraints
- **Risks**: Making a bad decision based on incomplete information, missing a competitive threat, overpaying for an acquisition

Trigger questions for customer pains:
- "What does the client complain about when using MI services?"
- "What frustrations do they have with current solutions (including doing it themselves)?"
- "What risks keep them up at night related to the decisions our work informs?"
- "What common mistakes do they make when they lack good intelligence?"
- "What barriers prevent them from getting this job done today?"

**Customer Gains:** What outcomes and benefits the client wants or would be delighted by.

- **Required gains**: The baseline expectation. "Accurate data." "On time."
- **Expected gains**: What they assume they will get. "Clear analysis with recommendations."
- **Desired gains**: What they would love but do not expect. "Ongoing strategic counsel." "Introductions to relevant contacts."
- **Unexpected gains**: Beyond anything they imagine. "A framework they can reuse internally." "Competitive intelligence system they can maintain."

Trigger questions for customer gains:
- "What would make the client's day related to this job?"
- "What would exceed their expectations?"
- "How do they measure success for this type of work?"
- "What would make them eager to refer you to their network?"

### Value Map (Left Side)

Design the offering to address the customer profile.

**Products and Services:** List the specific offerings CTRS provides or could provide to this segment.
- Rank by importance to the customer job (essential vs. nice-to-have)
- Categorize: physical (reports, dashboards), intangible (expertise, advice), digital (platforms, tools), financial (pricing models, guarantees)

**Pain Relievers:** How each product or service alleviates a specific customer pain.
- Map each pain reliever to a specific pain from the Customer Profile
- Be concrete: "Delivers findings in 2 weeks vs. industry standard of 6 weeks" (addresses the pain of slow turnaround)
- Rank by intensity of pain relief (does it eliminate the pain or just reduce it?)

**Gain Creators:** How each product or service creates a specific customer gain.
- Map each gain creator to a specific gain from the Customer Profile
- Be concrete: "Includes a quarterly competitive update for 12 months" (creates the desired gain of ongoing intelligence)
- Rank by relevance and impact

### The Three Types of Fit

**Fit Level 1: Problem-Solution Fit (on paper)**
- The Value Map addresses the most important jobs, most severe pains, and most desired gains from the Customer Profile.
- Evidence: Logical coherence. Customer interviews confirm that the jobs, pains, and gains are real and significant.
- Test: "Do customers say they have this problem, and does our design address it?"

**Fit Level 2: Product-Market Fit (in the market)**
- Clients are actually buying and using the service. Evidence of traction.
- Evidence: Revenue, repeat business, inbound demand, referrals.
- Test: "Are clients paying for this, coming back, and telling others?"

**Fit Level 3: Business Model Fit (at scale)**
- The value proposition is embedded in a profitable, scalable business model.
- Evidence: Healthy unit economics, growing client base, sustainable operations.
- Test: "Can we deliver this profitably at increasing volume?"

Move through these sequentially. Do not invest in scaling (Fit 3) until Product-Market Fit (Fit 2) is confirmed. Do not invest in building (Fit 2) until Problem-Solution Fit (Fit 1) is validated.

## 5. Business Model Canvas

Source: *Business Model Generation* (Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur)

A one-page view of how the business creates, delivers, and captures value. Use this to design, test, and iterate the business model. All nine blocks must be coherent with each other.

### The Nine Building Blocks

**1. Customer Segments:** Who are the distinct groups of people or organizations CTRS serves?
- Define segments by distinct needs, behaviors, or attributes.
- For each segment: Are they a mass market, niche market, segmented, diversified, or multi-sided platform?
- Key question: "Which segments are most valuable and most aligned with our strategy?"

**2. Value Propositions:** What bundle of products and services creates value for each segment?
- The specific mix of elements that solves a client problem or satisfies a need.
- Elements: Newness, Performance, Customization, Getting the Job Done, Design, Brand/Status, Price, Cost Reduction, Risk Reduction, Accessibility, Convenience.
- Key question: "Why does this segment choose us over alternatives?"

**3. Channels:** How does CTRS reach, communicate with, and deliver value to each segment?
- Channel phases: Awareness (how do they learn about us?), Evaluation (how do they assess us?), Purchase (how do they buy?), Delivery (how do we deliver?), After-sales (how do we support?).
- Own channels vs. partner channels. Direct vs. indirect.
- Key question: "What is the most effective and efficient way to reach each segment?"

**4. Customer Relationships:** What type of relationship does each segment expect?
- Types: Dedicated personal assistance, Self-service, Automated services, Communities, Co-creation.
- For a MI firm, relationships typically range from transactional (single project) to deep advisory (ongoing retainer with strategic counsel).
- Key question: "What relationship type maximizes lifetime value for each segment?"

**5. Revenue Streams:** How does CTRS generate revenue from each segment?
- Types: Project fees, Retainer/subscription, Licensing, Usage-based, Performance-based.
- Pricing mechanisms: Fixed menu, Negotiated, Yield management, Market-dependent.
- Key question: "What are clients truly willing to pay for, and how do they prefer to pay?"

**6. Key Resources:** What assets are essential to make the business model work?
- Physical: Office, technology infrastructure.
- Intellectual: Proprietary methods, frameworks, databases, brand, partnerships.
- Human: Analysts, domain experts, relationship managers, leaders.
- Financial: Cash, credit, revenue backlog.
- Key question: "Which resources are most critical and hardest to replicate?"

**7. Key Activities:** What essential things must CTRS do to make the model work?
- For a MI firm: Research and analysis, client management, business development, methodology development, knowledge management, talent development.
- Key question: "What activities directly create or deliver the value proposition?"

**8. Key Partnerships:** Who are the essential partners and suppliers?
- Types: Strategic alliances (non-competitors), Coopetition (competitors partnering), Joint ventures, Buyer-supplier relationships.
- Reasons: Optimization/scale, Risk reduction, Resource acquisition.
- For a MI firm: Data providers, technology partners, subcontractor specialists, referral partners, academic institutions.
- Key question: "What can partners do better or cheaper than we can?"

**9. Cost Structure:** What are the most important costs in operating the model?
- Cost-driven vs. value-driven. A differentiated MI firm is typically value-driven (optimizing for quality, not minimum cost).
- Fixed costs: Salaries, office, subscriptions, tools.
- Variable costs: Subcontractors, data purchases, travel, project-specific expenses.
- Key question: "Which costs are essential to the value proposition and which are wasteful?"

### Using the Canvas

- Fill it out on one page. Physical or digital, but keep it visual and compact.
- Build one canvas per business model variant being evaluated.
- Test coherence: Do the right side (revenue/customers) and left side (cost/infrastructure) tell a consistent story?
- Iterate: The canvas is a living document, not a one-time exercise.

## 6. Moore's Beachhead Strategy

Source: *Crossing the Chasm* (Geoffrey Moore)

Although written for technology products, the core framework applies to any firm trying to establish a dominant position in a new market segment. For CTRS, use this when entering a new vertical, launching a new service line, or targeting a new client type.

### The Chasm Concept

Markets adopt new offerings in a predictable sequence: Innovators, Early Adopters, Early Majority, Late Majority, Laggards. Between Early Adopters and Early Majority lies a "chasm" -- a dangerous gap where many offerings die.

- **Early Adopters** buy because they see strategic advantage in something new. They tolerate imperfection. They are visionaries.
- **Early Majority** buy because they see proven value that others like them are already getting. They want references, case studies, and a complete solution. They are pragmatists.

The chasm exists because pragmatists do not trust visionaries as references. You need to win an entire segment of pragmatists -- a beachhead -- to create the references that unlock the broader market.

### The D-Day Analogy

Treat market entry like a military invasion:

1. **Pick a single, narrowly defined beachhead** -- one segment where you can achieve dominance.
2. **Focus all resources on that beachhead** -- do not spread across multiple segments simultaneously.
3. **Win it completely** -- become the undisputed leader for that segment.
4. **Use the beachhead as a base** to expand into adjacent segments.

For CTRS: Do not try to serve "mid-market companies that need market intelligence." Pick one specific segment (e.g., "Series B-C fintech companies evaluating market expansion") and dominate it before expanding.

### Target Customer Characterization (Scenario Method)

Define the target customer using a concrete scenario, not demographics. Write a one-page scenario that includes:

1. **A day in the life (before)**: What does the target customer's work life look like without your offering? What are they struggling with? What workaround are they using?
2. **A day in the life (after)**: What does it look like after they adopt your offering? How is their life better?
3. **The scene**: A specific situation where the pain is acute and the value of your offering is obvious.

Template:
- "It's [time of year] and [buyer persona] at [type of company] is facing [specific challenge]. They currently [workaround]. This results in [negative consequences]. With CTRS, they would [better outcome], because [specific value delivered]."

### The 9-Factor Market Development Strategy Checklist

Use this to score and compare potential beachhead segments. Rate each factor 1-5 for each candidate segment.

| # | Factor | What to Assess |
|---|--------|---------------|
| 1 | Target customer | Is there a single, identifiable, economic buyer? |
| 2 | Compelling reason to buy | Is there an urgent, high-stakes problem? |
| 3 | Whole product | Can we deliver a complete solution (not just the core service)? |
| 4 | Competition | How entrenched is the competition in this segment? |
| 5 | Partners and allies | Can we partner to deliver the whole product? |
| 6 | Distribution | Can we reach and sell to this segment through existing channels? |
| 7 | Pricing | Can we price at levels that support a sustainable business? |
| 8 | Positioning | Can we credibly position as the leader for this segment? |
| 9 | Next target customer | Does winning this segment open doors to adjacent segments? |

Sum the scores. The highest-scoring segment is the strongest beachhead candidate. But also gut-check: Can you win a reference client in this segment within 90 days?

### The Whole Product Model

The core offering alone is not enough to win pragmatists. Define and deliver the "whole product":

- **Generic product**: The core service you deliver (e.g., a competitive analysis report).
- **Expected product**: What the client expects beyond the core (e.g., delivered on time, in a usable format, with a presentation walkthrough).
- **Augmented product**: The additional services that complete the experience (e.g., a follow-up Q&A session, integration with the client's strategic planning process, a summary for the board).
- **Potential product**: Room for growth and customization (e.g., ongoing monitoring, custom dashboards, advisory retainer).

Ask: "What would the client need beyond our core deliverable to get the full value from our work? Who provides that today, and should we provide it or partner for it?"

### The Bowling Pin Model

Expansion strategy after securing the beachhead:

1. **Pin 1 (beachhead)**: Dominate the first segment.
2. **Adjacent pins**: Identify segments that share characteristics with the beachhead -- similar buyer, similar problem, similar industry, or overlapping networks.
3. **Knock down pins in sequence**: Use references and case studies from Pin 1 to win Pin 2, then use both to win Pin 3.
4. **Build toward the bowling alley**: A series of vertical niches where you are the dominant specialist.
5. **Eventually reach the tornado**: Broad market adoption where demand exceeds supply.

For CTRS, map out 5-8 potential segment "pins" and sequence them based on adjacency to the beachhead.

## 7. Christensen's Disruption Frameworks

Source: *The Innovator's Dilemma*, *The Innovator's Solution*, *Competing Against Luck* (Clayton Christensen)

### Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation

**Sustaining innovation** improves existing offerings along dimensions that current customers value. More depth, more rigor, faster delivery, better visualization. Most MI firms compete on sustaining innovation.

**Disruptive innovation** introduces offerings that are initially inferior on traditional dimensions but are simpler, more accessible, or more affordable -- and serve non-consumers or over-served customers.

Two types of disruption:
- **Low-end disruption**: Target over-served customers who are paying for more than they need. Example: Self-serve data platforms that replace expensive custom research for routine competitive monitoring.
- **New-market disruption**: Target non-consumers -- people who could not access the offering before. Example: AI-powered market intelligence tools that give startups access to insights previously affordable only by large enterprises.

**Diagnostic questions for CTRS:**
- "Are any competitors offering simpler, cheaper versions of what we do? Are they gaining traction?"
- "Are any of our clients over-served -- paying for depth or rigor they do not use?"
- "Are there potential clients who cannot afford MI services today but would benefit from a simpler version?"
- "Is technology enabling new entrants to deliver 'good enough' intelligence at dramatically lower cost?"

### The RPV Framework (Resources, Processes, Values)

Use this to assess whether CTRS can execute a new strategic direction.

**Resources:** What does CTRS have? Tangible (people, money, technology, data) and intangible (brand, relationships, expertise, IP). Resources are the most flexible -- they can be acquired, hired, or built.

**Processes:** How does CTRS do things? Research methodology, client engagement model, quality assurance, knowledge management, hiring/onboarding. Processes are efficient for what they were designed to do, but they resist doing new things. A process optimized for $50K custom engagements will struggle to deliver $5K productized reports.

**Values:** What does CTRS prioritize in decision-making? Which clients matter, which engagements are worthwhile, what margins are acceptable? Values determine what the organization will and will not pursue. If CTRS values high-touch custom work, it will instinctively resist standardized products even if they are strategically correct.

**Key insight:** When a new strategy requires different processes and values (not just different resources), it is very difficult to execute within the existing organization. Consider whether the new initiative needs its own structure, team, or operating model.

Questions:
- "Do we have the resources to pursue this? If not, can we acquire them?"
- "Do our current processes support this, or will they fight it?"
- "Does our organizational culture and decision-making value this type of work, or will it be deprioritized?"

### Discovery-Driven Planning

Use this instead of traditional planning when entering uncertain territory. Traditional planning assumes the forecasts are right and builds from there. Discovery-Driven Planning assumes the forecasts are wrong and focuses on testing them.

**Step 1: Define the required outcomes.** What revenue, profit, and ROI must this initiative deliver to justify the investment?

**Step 2: Create a reverse income statement.** Work backward from the required profit to the revenue, costs, and unit economics needed to get there.

**Step 3: List all assumptions.** Every number in the reverse income statement is an assumption. List them all. Be explicit. Examples: "Clients will pay $X per engagement," "We can deliver in Y hours," "Z% of proposals will convert."

**Step 4: Rank assumptions by importance and uncertainty.** Which assumptions, if wrong, would kill the initiative? Which are you least sure about?

**Step 5: Plan to test assumptions at milestones.** Set checkpoints where you test the most critical assumptions. Budget for learning, not for execution. Each milestone is a go/no-go decision point.

**Step 6: Manage the initiative through checkpoints.** At each checkpoint, update assumptions with real data. Adjust the plan or kill the initiative based on what you learn.

### Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD)

Clients do not buy services. They "hire" services to make progress in a specific circumstance.

**The framework:**
- "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."
- Example: "When my CEO asks me to evaluate a new market before the board meeting in 3 weeks, I want to get a credible market analysis fast, so I can present a data-backed recommendation and not look unprepared."

**How to uncover jobs:**
1. Interview recent clients. Ask about the circumstance, not the service: "Walk me through what was happening when you decided to look for help."
2. Look for workarounds: What were they doing before they hired you? Cobbling together analyst reports? Asking their network? Guessing?
3. Identify the functional, social, and emotional dimensions of the job.
4. Map the competition: Who else gets "hired" for this job? (Not just MI competitors -- internal teams, consultants, data platforms, doing nothing.)

**Apply JTBD to CTRS:**
- "What are the 3-5 jobs that CTRS gets hired to do most often?"
- "Are we organized around the job or around our service offering?"
- "Could we redesign our service to make progress on the job faster, more reliably, or more completely?"

## 8. Lean Startup for Strategy Testing

Source: *The Lean Startup* (Eric Ries)

Use these tools whenever CTRS is testing a new strategic direction, service line, market, or business model. Do not make large commitments to untested strategies.

### Build-Measure-Learn Loop

The core cycle. Run it as fast as possible.

1. **Build**: Create the minimum viable version of the offering, message, or experiment.
2. **Measure**: Collect data on whether the hypothesis holds. Use actionable, accessible, auditable metrics.
3. **Learn**: Determine whether to persevere, pivot, or kill. Update the strategy based on evidence.

**Speed matters.** The total time through the loop -- not the quality of any individual step -- determines how fast CTRS learns and adapts.

### MVP Types for a Services Firm

Choose the lightest-weight MVP that tests the critical hypothesis:

| MVP Type | What It Tests | Example for CTRS |
|----------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Concierge MVP** | Whether clients value the outcome (fully manual, one client at a time) | Hand-deliver a custom intelligence briefing to one prospect, doing everything manually. |
| **Wizard of Oz MVP** | Whether the experience works (looks automated but is manual behind the scenes) | Offer a "dashboard" that is actually a manually updated spreadsheet with a nice front end. |
| **Landing page MVP** | Whether there is demand for the offering | Create a page describing a new service and measure sign-ups or inquiries. |
| **Single-feature MVP** | Whether the core value proposition works | Deliver only the competitive analysis component, not the full market assessment. |
| **Smoke test** | Whether the market exists | Run ads or send emails describing the new offering and measure response. |
| **Piecemeal MVP** | Whether you can deliver without building infrastructure | Combine existing tools and services to approximate the new offering. |

### Innovation Accounting (Three Steps)

Standard financial metrics (revenue, profit) are lagging indicators that do not tell you whether a new initiative is on the right track. Use Innovation Accounting instead.

**Step 1: Establish the baseline.**
- Build a basic MVP and measure the current state of key metrics.
- For CTRS: What is the conversion rate, engagement rate, or satisfaction score for the new offering at launch?

**Step 2: Tune the engine.**
- Run experiments to move the baseline metrics toward the target.
- Each experiment should change one variable and measure the impact.
- For CTRS: Test different positioning, pricing, delivery format, scope, or target buyer.

**Step 3: Pivot or persevere.**
- If experiments are moving the metrics toward the target, persevere.
- If experiments are not moving the metrics despite significant effort, pivot.
- Pivoting is not failure. It is a strategic course correction based on evidence.

### The 10 Pivot Types

When the current approach is not working, choose the type of pivot that best addresses what you have learned:

1. **Zoom-in pivot**: A single feature or component of the service becomes the whole offering. (The competitive benchmarking section becomes a standalone product.)
2. **Zoom-out pivot**: The current offering becomes one component of a larger offering. (The market report becomes part of a strategic advisory retainer.)
3. **Customer segment pivot**: The offering is valuable, but for a different segment than expected. (Built for corporates, but PE firms are the real buyers.)
4. **Customer need pivot**: The problem you're solving is not important enough; pivot to a related but more urgent problem the same customer has.
5. **Platform pivot**: Shift from a single service to a platform, or from a platform to a single service.
6. **Business architecture pivot**: Shift from high-margin/low-volume to low-margin/high-volume (or vice versa).
7. **Value capture pivot**: Change how you monetize. (Shift from project fees to subscription. Shift from hourly to value-based pricing.)
8. **Engine of growth pivot**: Shift between sticky growth (retention), viral growth (referral), and paid growth (acquisition).
9. **Channel pivot**: Change how you deliver the offering or reach the client.
10. **Technology pivot**: Use a different technology to deliver the same value proposition.

### Leap-of-Faith Assumptions

Every new strategy rests on two fundamental assumptions that must be tested first:

**Value hypothesis**: Does the offering deliver value to the client? Will they use it, benefit from it, come back for more?
- Test by: Delivering to early clients and measuring engagement, satisfaction, repeat purchase, willingness to pay.

**Growth hypothesis**: How will new clients discover the offering? Will it spread?
- Test by: Measuring referral rates, organic inbound, conversion rates from marketing, retention curves.

Identify these two assumptions explicitly for any new initiative. Test the value hypothesis first -- there is no point testing growth for something that does not deliver value.

## 9. Collins' Core Ideology

Source: *Built to Last* and *Good to Great* (Jim Collins and Jerry Porras)

### Preserve the Core / Stimulate Progress

The fundamental dynamic of enduring organizations: they maintain an unwavering core identity while relentlessly changing and adapting everything else.

- **Core ideology** (preserve): Core values + core purpose. These do not change. They are discovered, not invented. They exist independent of market conditions, competitive dynamics, or management fashions.
- **Everything else** (stimulate progress): Strategy, tactics, offerings, structures, systems, processes, culture practices. All of this should evolve constantly.

**The critical distinction:** Core ideology is not a strategy. It does not tell you where to compete or how to win. It tells you who you are regardless of those choices.

### Core Values Discovery: The Mars Group Method

Do not brainstorm values. Discover them through the following process:

**Step 1:** Assemble a "Mars Group" -- a small group (3-7 people) who deeply embody the company at its best. If CTRS is small, this may be the founder and 1-2 key people.

**Step 2:** Ask each member independently: "Who are the 3-5 people in this organization who, in your experience, most embody the company at its best? What makes them exemplary?"

**Step 3:** From these exemplars, extract the qualities, behaviors, and principles they demonstrate. Look for patterns across the nominations.

**Step 4:** Propose candidate core values (3-5 maximum). For each candidate, apply these tests:

- **The "Would you keep it?" test**: "If circumstances changed such that this value became a competitive disadvantage, would we still keep it?" If no, it is not a core value -- it is a strategy.
- **The "Mars Group" test**: "If you were starting a new company on a different planet with a different product, would you bring this value?" If yes, it is core.
- **The "100 years" test**: "Can you envision this being as valid 100 years from now as it is today?"
- **The "authenticity" test**: "Do we already live this value, even imperfectly? Or is it aspirational?" Core values are descriptive (who we are), not aspirational (who we wish we were).

**Step 5:** Finalize 3-5 core values. Fewer is better. Each should be a short phrase with a brief explanatory paragraph.

### Core Purpose Discovery: The Five Whys Method

Core purpose is the organization's reason for being beyond making money. It is idealistic and enduring.

**Process:**
1. Start with a description of what the company does: "We provide market intelligence consulting."
2. Ask "Why is that important?" Answer: "Because clients need better information to make decisions."
3. Ask "Why is THAT important?" Answer: "Because better decisions lead to better outcomes for their businesses."
4. Ask "Why is THAT important?" Answer: "Because when companies thrive, they create value for employees, customers, and communities."
5. Ask "Why is THAT important?" Answer: "Because we believe organizations can be a force for progress when they see the world clearly."

After 4-5 rounds, you approach something that sounds like a purpose. It should be motivating, enduring, and bigger than any single strategy.

Test: "Would this purpose remain valid if we changed industries entirely?"

### BHAGs: Big Hairy Audacious Goals

A BHAG is a 10-25 year goal that is clear, compelling, and serves as a unifying focal point. It is NOT a core value or purpose -- it is a mechanism to stimulate progress.

**Four categories of BHAGs:**

1. **Target BHAGs**: A clear quantitative or definitive target. "Become a $10M firm by 2035." "Be the recognized leader in market intelligence for the healthcare sector."

2. **Common-enemy BHAGs**: Define a competitor or category to beat. "Become the go-to alternative to Big Four consulting for mid-market competitive intelligence."

3. **Role-model BHAGs**: "Become the McKinsey of market intelligence for the mid-market" or "Become the [admired company] of [our space]."

4. **Internal-transformation BHAGs**: Transform some aspect of the organization. "Build a firm where every analyst is a recognized expert who publishes original research."

**A good BHAG should:**
- Take 10-25 years to achieve
- Be clear enough that you know when you've hit it
- Require significant growth and change
- Feel 50-70% achievable (not certain, not impossible)
- Be motivating regardless of who is leading the firm

### The Genius of the AND

Reject the "Tyranny of the OR" -- the belief that things must be either/or. Enduring organizations embrace paradox:

- Purpose AND profit
- Continuity AND change
- Freedom AND responsibility
- Discipline AND creativity
- Humility AND fierce resolve

For CTRS: Do not accept false trade-offs.
- "We can be boutique AND scalable."
- "We can be rigorous AND fast."
- "We can be specialized AND adaptable."
- "We can serve clients AND build intellectual property."

When someone presents an either/or choice, ask: "How could we do both?"

## 10. Reeves' Strategy Palette

Source: *Your Strategy Needs a Strategy* (Martin Reeves, Knut Haanaes, Janmejaya Sinha)

Not all strategic environments are the same. The right approach depends on two dimensions: **predictability** (can you forecast the future of your market?) and **malleability** (can you shape the future of your market?).

### The Five Approaches

#### Classical (Predictable, Not Malleable)

**When to use:** The competitive environment is relatively stable and predictable. You can analyze the market, develop a plan, and execute it.

**How to execute:**
1. Analyze the industry structure (Porter's Five Forces, PESTEL).
2. Build a sustainable competitive advantage through positioning.
3. Develop a detailed plan with clear milestones.
4. Execute with discipline over multi-year horizons.
5. Scale the advantage.

**Motto:** "Be big." Pursue scale and positional advantage.

**For CTRS:** Use when operating in stable, well-understood segments where client needs and competitive dynamics are predictable. Plan deliberately and execute methodically.

#### Adaptive (Not Predictable, Not Malleable)

**When to use:** The environment is changing rapidly and unpredictably, and you cannot influence the direction. Turbulent, uncertain markets. New technologies disrupting the space. Rapid shifts in client needs.

**How to execute:**
1. Run continuous experiments -- many small bets, not one big one.
2. Select the experiments that work and scale them quickly.
3. Kill the experiments that fail, without hesitation.
4. Maintain strategic flexibility -- do not lock in to long-term commitments.
5. Build organizational agility: fast decision-making, cross-functional teams, rapid reallocation.

**Motto:** "Be fast." Speed of experimentation and adaptation beats quality of planning.

**For CTRS:** Use when the MI industry is being disrupted by AI tools, when client needs are shifting unpredictably, or when entering unfamiliar markets. Run many small experiments rather than one big strategic bet.

#### Visionary (Predictable, Malleable)

**When to use:** You can see a future that others cannot, and you have the ability to create it. A clear opportunity that is not yet recognized by the market.

**How to execute:**
1. Envision a possibility that does not yet exist.
2. Build the offering before demand is established.
3. Persist through early skepticism and slow adoption.
4. Be first and commit fully.
5. Scale quickly once the market follows.

**Motto:** "Be first." See the future and build it.

**For CTRS:** Use when you have a clear vision for a new way to deliver market intelligence that does not yet exist -- a new service model, a new technology application, a new client relationship structure. Commit and build before competitors see the opportunity.

#### Shaping (Not Predictable, Malleable)

**When to use:** The environment is uncertain, but you can influence its evolution. Emerging markets, new categories, platform plays, ecosystem building.

**How to execute:**
1. Engage others in a shared vision -- build an ecosystem, not just a company.
2. Create a platform or standard that others build on.
3. Collaborate with partners, even competitors, to grow the category.
4. Evolve the vision as the ecosystem develops.
5. Orchestrate the network, do not try to control it.

**Motto:** "Be the orchestrator." Shape the market collaboratively.

**For CTRS:** Use when building a new category of service (e.g., an intelligence-sharing consortium), creating partnerships that reshape how MI is delivered, or building a platform that other firms or clients build on.

#### Renewal (Harsh Environment)

**When to use:** The firm is in a hostile environment -- declining revenue, lost competitive position, resource constraints, survival mode. The current strategy is failing and resources are limited.

**How to execute:**
1. **React phase**: Cut costs, conserve cash, focus on survival. Honest diagnosis of what is broken.
2. **Economize**: Reduce complexity. Cut offerings, clients, and activities that are not essential. Preserve only what is core.
3. **Grow again**: Once stabilized, pivot to one of the other four approaches. Use the reduced base as a launchpad, not a permanent state.

**Motto:** "Be viable." Survive first, then grow.

**For CTRS:** Use when the firm has lost a major client, when revenue has dropped significantly, when cash is tight, or when the current strategy has clearly failed. Do not try to grow out of a crisis -- stabilize first.

### Ambidexterity: Combining Approaches

Mature firms often need to apply different approaches simultaneously to different parts of the business.

**Separation**: Run different parts of the business with different approaches. The core MI business may use a Classical approach while a new AI-powered product uses an Adaptive approach. Keep them structurally separate with different metrics, processes, and expectations.

**Switching**: Change approaches over time as the environment evolves. Start with Visionary for a new service, switch to Classical once the market stabilizes.

**Self-organizing**: Empower different teams to choose their own approach based on their specific market context.

**For CTRS at its size:** Full ambidexterity is structurally difficult with 2-10 people. Instead, sequence approaches: use Adaptive/Visionary for new initiatives with time-boxed experiments, and Classical for the established core. Be explicit about which approach applies to which initiative.

## 11. Ansoff's Growth Matrix

Source: *Corporate Strategy* (H. Igor Ansoff)

A simple framework for evaluating growth options along two dimensions: markets (existing vs. new) and products/services (existing vs. new). Risk increases as you move away from the current core.

### The Four Growth Strategies

#### Market Penetration (Existing Services, Existing Markets) -- Lowest Risk

Grow by selling more of the current services to the current client segments.

**Tactics for CTRS:**
- Increase share of wallet with existing clients (upsell, cross-sell, expand scope)
- Win clients from competitors in the same segments
- Increase engagement frequency (move from project-based to retainer)
- Improve retention and reduce churn
- Intensify marketing and business development in current segments

**When to prioritize:** When the current market is not saturated, when there is significant untapped demand in existing segments, when the offering is strong but awareness or reach is weak.

**Key question:** "Are we getting all the business we should from our current market with our current offering?"

#### Product/Service Development (New Services, Existing Markets) -- Moderate Risk

Grow by developing new services for the current client base.

**Tactics for CTRS:**
- Launch new service lines that address adjacent client needs (e.g., add strategic advisory to existing research)
- Productize existing capabilities (turn custom research into subscription intelligence)
- Add new delivery formats (dashboards, workshops, embedded analysts)
- Develop new methodologies or analytical frameworks
- Bundle existing services into new packages

**When to prioritize:** When current clients have needs you are not addressing, when competitors are winning adjacent work, when you have capabilities that are under-monetized.

**Key question:** "What else do our current clients need that we could provide?"

#### Market Development (Existing Services, New Markets) -- Moderate Risk

Grow by bringing the current services to new client segments.

**Tactics for CTRS:**
- Enter new industry verticals with existing MI capabilities
- Expand geographically
- Target a different buyer persona (e.g., shift from serving corporate strategy teams to serving PE firms)
- Target a different company size tier
- Enter new channels (partnerships, platforms, marketplaces)

**When to prioritize:** When the current market is saturated or shrinking, when the offering is proven and transferable, when adjacent markets have clear demand.

**Key question:** "Who else could benefit from what we already do well?"

#### Diversification (New Services, New Markets) -- Highest Risk

Grow by offering new services to new client segments. This is the riskiest path because you have no existing advantage in either dimension.

**Two types:**
- **Related diversification**: The new market/service is connected to existing capabilities or knowledge. (A MI firm launching a training/education business for corporate strategy teams.)
- **Unrelated diversification**: No connection to existing operations. (Generally inadvisable for a small firm.)

**When to prioritize:** Rarely, and only when related to existing capabilities. For CTRS, pursue only if the core business is mature and generating surplus resources, AND the diversification builds on existing strengths.

**Key question:** "Is this genuinely building on what we know, or are we just chasing a shiny object?"

### Using the Matrix

1. Map all current and potential growth initiatives into the four quadrants.
2. Assess the risk level of each initiative relative to CTRS's resources and risk tolerance.
3. Balance the portfolio: most investment should go to Penetration and one of the two moderate-risk quadrants. Limit diversification.
4. Sequence initiatives: pursue lower-risk opportunities first to build resources and capabilities for higher-risk moves.

## 12. Bet-David's Five Moves

Source: *Your Next Five Moves* (Patrick Bet-David)

A practical framework for strategic sequencing. Rather than reacting to circumstances, think five moves ahead like a chess master. Each move builds on the previous one.

### Move 1: Master Knowing Yourself

Before making strategic decisions, develop complete clarity on who you are, what you want, and what drives you.

**Actions for CTRS:**
- Define the founder's and firm's core identity. What type of firm do you want to build? A lifestyle business? A scalable enterprise? A platform?
- Clarify the "why" behind the business beyond revenue. What impact do you want to have?
- Identify personal strengths and weaknesses honestly. Where does the founder add the most value? Where are they a bottleneck?
- Define non-negotiables. What will you never compromise on, regardless of financial pressure?
- Audit your decision-making patterns. Where do you consistently make good decisions? Where do you repeatedly make bad ones?

**Key questions:**
- "What type of business am I actually building, vs. what type do I tell people I am building?"
- "In 10 years, what do I want this firm to look like? How many people? What revenue? What reputation?"
- "What decisions have I been avoiding? Why?"

### Move 2: Master the Ability to Reason

Develop the ability to process information, see cause and effect, and make sound strategic decisions under uncertainty.

**Actions for CTRS:**
- Build a decision-making framework. For every significant decision, map: What are the options? What are the second-order effects of each? What is the reversibility?
- Study the competitive landscape deeply. Understand not just who competitors are but how they think and what they will do next.
- Develop scenario planning capability. For any strategic move, play out the best-case, worst-case, and most-likely-case scenarios.
- Build the ability to think in terms of leverage points. Where does a small input create a disproportionate output?
- Process information from multiple sources before making calls. Seek disconfirming evidence, not just supporting evidence.

**Key questions:**
- "If we make this move, what will competitors do in response? And what will we do after that?"
- "What is the second-order effect of this decision that we are not thinking about?"
- "What information would change my mind about this? Where would I find it?"

### Move 3: Master Building the Right Team

Strategy is executed by people. Build a team that can execute the strategy.

**Actions for CTRS:**
- Define the 3-5 roles that are most critical to the strategy. Staff these positions with the best people you can find or develop.
- Understand what motivates each team member and align incentives with strategic priorities.
- Build a culture of accountability. Clear expectations, transparent metrics, honest feedback.
- Address team weaknesses before they become crises. The right people in the wrong seats, or the wrong people entirely, will undermine any strategy.
- Create a talent pipeline. Who are the next 2-3 hires that the strategy requires? Start building relationships now.

**Key questions:**
- "Do we have the right people to execute this strategy? If not, what is the plan?"
- "Who on the team needs to develop new capabilities for the strategy to work?"
- "Is anyone on the team misaligned with where the firm is going?"

### Move 4: Master Strategy to Scale

Design and execute the scaling strategy.

**Actions for CTRS:**
- Choose the growth path (reference Ansoff's Matrix) and commit to it.
- Build systems that allow growth without proportional increases in the founder's time. The founder is the most constrained resource.
- Develop repeatable processes for core activities: business development, engagement delivery, quality assurance, knowledge management.
- Invest in the infrastructure (technology, tools, processes) that will be needed at the next level of scale, not just the current level.
- Set clear targets and work backward to the actions required this quarter (connect to EOS Rocks methodology in `execution-implementation.md`).

**Key questions:**
- "What is the one bottleneck that, if removed, would allow the business to grow 2x?"
- "What am I doing today that someone else should be doing?"
- "What systems need to exist for the firm to function well when I am not involved in every engagement?"

### Move 5: Master Power Plays

Make bold strategic moves that create lasting competitive advantage.

**Actions for CTRS:**
- Identify and execute strategic partnerships that create access to new markets or capabilities.
- Make a bold positioning move -- become publicly known for something specific and valuable. Thought leadership, a signature methodology, a published framework.
- Create strategic leverage through intellectual property, data assets, or platform positions that compound over time.
- Build relationships with key players in target markets before you need them.
- When the opportunity arises, act decisively. The best strategic moves are made when you have prepared for them in advance.

**Key questions:**
- "What is the one bold move that would change our competitive position dramatically?"
- "What relationships, if established, would open doors that are currently closed?"
- "What asset could we build that would become more valuable over time and harder for competitors to replicate?"

### Sequencing the Five Moves

The five moves are sequential but also cyclical:

1. Start with self-knowledge (Move 1) -- this informs every other move.
2. Develop reasoning capability (Move 2) -- this improves every decision.
3. Build the team (Move 3) -- you cannot scale without the right people.
4. Design and execute the scaling strategy (Move 4) -- grow deliberately.
5. Make power plays (Move 5) -- create lasting advantage.

Revisit Move 1 regularly. As the firm evolves, the founder's role and aspirations may need to evolve too. Each cycle through the five moves should operate at a higher level than the last.
